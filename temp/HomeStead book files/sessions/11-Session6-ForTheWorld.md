# Session Six

### For the World: Leaving a Legacy in Society

#### Overview

> Then our sons will be like plants nurtured in their youth, our daughters like corner pillars carved to adorn a palace.

<< Psalm 144:12

> Youth is an ocean of dangers. Within, without, on every side they are beset with perils; the flesh is most strong and stirring, the world is most amiable and taking, besides, the wicked one is subtle and tempting, ever striving to hale them to hell ... Children are compared to arrows ... Now we know that sticks are not by nature arrows; they do not grow so, but they are made so; by nature they are knotty and ragged, but by art they are made smooth and handsome. So children by nature are rugged and untoward, but by education are refined and reformed, made pliable to the divine will and pleasure. They are also compared to corner-stones ... which are rough and unfit for use, as they are taken out of the quarry, and therefore must be hewn and carved before they are laid in the foundation.

<< George Swinnock, *The Christian Man's Calling*

Children are a gift from God. The sober responsibility of parenting children must be balanced with exuberant thanksgiving over the blessing of each child. Children bring joy to the present and represent a promise for the future. They signify God's favor and move us to deepened trust in God. They remind us of life's simplicity and press us into the profound. They make life full and offer a work that is fulfilling. They reflect us (in person, personality, and feature) but also urge us toward greater holiness of life and character. They receive the nurture from family only to eventually contribute to church, society, and world. God works out his purpose in the world through children. What we build on hearth and home—and what kind of children we launch into the world—leaves a lasting impact on the globe. In fact, to build Christian hearth and home according to God's design is one of the great ways to participate in God's mission in the world. This is one of the rich blessings of family life and children. Godly families raise children in the faith and send them out into the world as salt and light. Like arrows, children are shaped, sharpened, and sent into the world. Like stones, children are honed, carved, and established as part of God's people. With our families before us, we are servants of God, tending to our children as God's vessels. We build our homes to build God's kingdom. We sharpen our children to serve God's work in the world.

#### Key Idea

As overseers, we will build Christian hearth and home as a heritage from the Lord.

### Seeking God's Wisdom

*Focus your thinking around the key idea and biblical wisdom. Review the introduction and discuss the scriptural proverb to help orient you to the main theme of this session.*

#### Introduction

> The bows of the mighty are broken, but the feeble are equipped with strength. The well-fed hire themselves out for food, but the starving hunger no more. The barren woman gives birth to seven, but she who has many sons pines away.

<< 1 Samuel 2:4–5

> Observe here, Children of the youth are arrows in the hand, which, with prudence, may be directed aright to the mark, God's glory and the service of their generation; but afterwards, when they have gone abroad into the world, they are arrows out of the hand; it is too late to bend them then. But these arrows in the hand too often prove arrows in the heart, a constant grief to their godly parents, whose gray hairs they bring with sorrow to the grave.

<< Matthew Henry, *Commentary*

God is working his purpose out. His will is guaranteed to come to fruition. He has an eternal plan to restore his fallen creation and redeem his people for himself. God is unhindered in this work. In his eternal plan, God will triumph over evil and vanquish all of his enemies, forgiving sinners, saving the lost, and restoring fallen creation to everlasting bliss. As we experience God's saving grace in our lives, he invites us to join his mission in the world. To accomplish his grand and glorious purpose, God sent his Son, Jesus Christ, into the world. Jesus is God's one and only Son, who accomplished for humanity a work that he alone could perform. Nevertheless, as believers in Christ, one way for us to participate in God's mission is for us to send our sons and daughters into the world ready to serve God, follow his Son, and do his will. This puts parenting work in the center of God's mission. As we experience the blessing of children, we should view Christian homebuilding as a way of participating in God's global work. In raising godly children, sharpening them as arrows and honing them like stones, we can change the world—for the gospel and for God's glory.

#### Biblical Wisdom

By wisdom a house is built and by understanding it is established; through knowledge its rooms are filled with every precious and beautiful treasure.

<< Proverbs 24:3–4

##### Core Principle

Parenting is homebuilding work. This proverb reminds us that we need prudence to build our homes after God's design: "by wisdom a house is built and by understanding it is established." In short, a strong, mature, and established home is rooted in biblical wisdom. Parents need wisdom to lay the foundation of a godly family. In addition, parents need to instill wisdom to their children. But parenting is more than just building a strong home. It aims for a flourishing family. The flourishing of the godly family is one of its great testimonies in the surrounding world. This proverbs tells us about a house built by wisdom: "through knowledge its rooms are filled with every precious and beautiful treasure." To build Christian hearth and home God's way is to experience the promise of divine favor in abundance. We not only need God's truth to understand the blueprint of a godly family; we also need God's strength to build our home in God's way..

#### Of Arrows, Quivers, and Olive Shoots

> O house of Israel, bless the Lord; O house of Aaron, bless the Lord; O house of Levi, bless the Lord; you who fear the Lord, bless the Lord! Blessed be the Lord from Zion— He who dwells in Jerusalem. Hallelujah!

<< Psalm 135:19–21

> Every Christian family ought to be as it were a little church, consecrated to Christ, and wholly influenced and governed by his rules.

<< Jonathan Edwards, *Farewell Sermon*

##### Introduction

All families have a purpose. A family's values are shaped by this purpose, creating a sort of household culture. Misguided purposes and values can corrupt a family's life and do damage to a child's formation. But godly aims can sanctify and prune a family to be a sharpened tool in the hand of God. Recognizing that family's have a purpose is an important first step. A brief glance at a family' budget (how they spend their money), calendar (how they spend their time), and conversation (what they talk about) all serve as a barometers, revealing the overarching purpose of that household. Family habits and traditions also reveal and expose a family's mission. The guests we entertain and the friends we share life with also give us a clue into what our families are all about—their driving cause and reason for existence. Just like all humans live for something, all families operate their collective life by some underling purpose.

<Callout>In order to build Christian hearth and home in a godly way, we must be grounded in godly purpose and mission.</Callout> God has given us the blessing of family not to be hoarded and clutched for our own enjoyment. The gift of family life is only realized to its fullest as we enlist our families in God's global work in the world. The portrait of a pious home in Psalm 127:1–5 and 128:1–6 helps us understand what values, purposes, and aims should define family life. As we build Christian hearth and home, we must understand that God is building a house for himself. This is not a house of brick and mortar, wood and stone. He is building a house of his people. Amazingly, our homebuilding efforts can contribute to this great work of God in the world. Let us attend to these coupled psalms with open hearts, wise minds, and ready hands as we consider what it means to place our homes in the establishing, safekeeping, and benevolent hands of our trustworthy Lord.

##### Building the House: Viewing Homebuilding as Meaningful Work

Establishing a home is good and godly work. The opening of the psalm highlights and honors the work of building a house (127:1). In the Old Testament, the idea of a house can speak about a physical dwelling place, a family unit, an entire ancestry, and the temple (the dwelling place of God where he met with his people). In biblical thought and in the Christian life, these are all connected. Part of God's overall mission is to redeem and build a people for his name. The building of God's people takes place through godly family units that extend their faith influence across generations. The building of a godly house is intensely spiritual work: raising godly children, cultivating a Christ-centered home culture, fortifying God's covenant people, and influencing wider society.

In building a house God's way, we hope to experience a fruitful and flourishing home. The great fruitfulness of a thriving home is not food that we eat but the children we bear. In this portion of the Bible, children are compared to "olive shoots" who come from the "fruitful vine" of the blessed and pious wife (128:3). God's blessing expressed in the form of children is considered both "the fruit of the womb" and God's "reward" to his people (127:3). But the godly family exists for the prosperity of something beyond its own household unit. In Psalm 127:1, the building of the house is parallel with the protection of the city. Psalm 128:5–6 attests that the long life of the God-fearer is gifted with seeing "the prosperity of Jerusalem" and his "children's children." In these psalms the building of the family is parallel with the secure establishing of the city—the collective of families that form the community. <Callout>Building hearth and home is set within the larger endeavor of establishing God's people and securing the best for larger society.</Callout>

Importantly, the text points out the vanity of trying to do homebuilding in our own efforts. God alone makes our efforts meaningful and purposeful. This is not saying that human work is in vain. It says that human work without the Lord's undertaking is in vain. Builders still should "labor" (127:1) and watchmen should still "stand guard" (127:1). Diligent work may still cause us to "rise early and stay up late" (127:2). The difference is this: those who fear the Lord do not do their work in anxious toil. Those who give themselves to the Lord in wholehearted trust can enjoy God's provision and "eat the fruit of your labor" (128:2). The faithful know that the building of the home, the security of the city, the fruitfulness of one's work is dependent upon God's help and enablement.

While these psalms speak about a meaningful work in general—honoring the dignity of all good vocations—the biblical texts are extremely familycentric. Certainly, believers should seek good work: building, securing, watching, toiling, and making (127:1–2; 128:2). The labor of our hands matters to the Lord. In fact, God has prepared good work for his people. Our job is to walk in the work that God has prepared in advance for us. When we consider the full scope of our lifework—the total work of our lives that we offer to the Lord—one of the most signifiant things we can do is to build godly families. The psalms attest to that fact that healthy and holy families are a core element for all of the other work that we do. Godly families give work meaning, help work endure, and make work enjoyable. <Callout>Building a godly household is one of the main ways we can have influence in God's covenant people and in surrounding world.</Callout>

##### Unless the Lord: Establishing Deep Dependence upon the Lord

Both psalms invite families into a deep devotion to God. The repeated refrain "unless the Lord" (127:1) is a statement of faith as it is of fact. No household can be established, just like no city can be secured, unless the Lord does the work. God's people are marked by a wholehearted reliance upon God. This is more than external religious show. Genuine trust is an enduring quality because it is an internal reality. Utter dependence upon the Lord is born out of the fear of the Lord. Not only will God's blessing be upon "the man who fears the Lord" (128:4), but divine benevolence will extend to "all who fear the Lord" (128:1). Sincere faith expressed in the fear of God reforms our conduct so that we "walk in His ways" (128:1).

Better than decorating a home with material embellishments is to furnish a home with fear of the Lord—a husband who leads the home out of a vibrant personal faith in the Lord, a wife who is fruitful in home and faithful in devotion to God, and children who trust, love, worship, and obey God. <Callout>The personal faith of the individual members of a household make up the real collective character of a home.</Callout> Equally, individual expressions of faith are molded by the family's overall culture of God-fearing devotion to God. Exemplified by a godly father, modeled by a pious mother, and embraced by dutiful children, the fear of God underlies all other God-honoring family activity. If a family wants to be oriented to God, impactful in the world, and blessing for generations to come, it begins with this most important beginning of personal and household faith.

##### May the Lord Bless You: Enjoying Divine Favor, Full Peace, and True Prosperity

The fear of the Lord puts us in the path to receive God's blessing (127:5; 128:1–2, 4–5) and guards us from vain pursuits (127:1–2), anxious toil (127:2), shameful scorn (127:5), and hopeless need (128:2, 5). Those who entrust themselves to the Lord in wholehearted trust experience divinely given rest: "He gives sleep to His beloved" (127:2). God causes them to flourish (128:3) and extends to him his goodness: "blessings and prosperity will be yours" (128:2; cf. 128:5). Faithful God-fearers also enjoy God's promise of peace: "peace be upon Israel" (128:6). While God's people enjoy his blessings in all areas of life, God's blessings on are particularly experienced within the home: "your wife will be like a fruitful vine flourishing within your house, your sons like olive shoots sitting around your table" (128:3).

It is easy for families to get entangled in worldly pursuits. Surrounding culture endlessly allures people toward its broken version of the good life. Its great marketing strategy is pleasure, enjoyment, and happiness. But it cannot deliver on what it promises. The world's vision of the good life is a crooked path that will lead to destruction. One of its focused targets is families and children. Surrounded by ideologies of the world, parents can feel pressure to acquiesce to the world's version of the good life. Without thinking, families can embrace a pace of life, invest in a flurry of activities, spend an inordinate amount of money all in pursuit of worldly happiness. Parents can compare themselves with other families and measure their parenting worth by the earthly possessions and temporal experiences they give their children. But it is a fool's errand to chase elusive and disappointing delights of the world. Instead, we ought to seek God's blessing. A family devoted to the Lord can enjoy true prosperity: enduring peace, profound joy, sustained rest, and everlasting life. Godly families forsake the pursuit of worldly happiness to embrace the superior offer of divine blessing.

##### When He Confronts the Enemies: Treating Family Life as a Battlefront Combat

Psalm 127 concludes by saying that a full and flourishing home will be a man's honor and defense in the public sector: "he will not be put to shame when he confronts the enemies at the gate" (127:5). Because there is a moral, religious, and spiritual conflict that exists in this fallen world, the life of faith is depicted as warfare. In a society opposed to God, God's people must prepare themselves to face enemies and opposition. This psalm attests that godly families are one of the greatest weapons in the fight of the faith. Children are compared to "arrows in the hand of a warrior" (127:4). The man who is not ashamed is the man "whose quiver is full of them" (127:5). The man who has "children born in one's youth" (127:4) is depicted as a man of honor, vigor, and valor. While having many children is a true blessing from God, Psalms 127–128 put together refuse for us to turn our children into idols. A family dedicated to the Lord—along with children who fear God (cf. 128:1, 4)—are considered God's beloved (127:2). God is working through godly families for something bigger than the family. As arrows are meant to be launched into the world (and in particular, released in battle), a godly home raises children not only as a heritage but as a weapon for the Lord's work.

<Callout>A well-ordered home aligned after God's design is one of our greatest testimonies in and defenses against a godless society.</Callout> As Christians, we confront godless culture by building godly families. We invest in a hopeful future by raising Christian children. We battle against a lost society—expressed in moral deviance, false doctrine, gross injustice, errant ideology, and distorted worldview—by cultivating the fear of God in the home. We bring about transformation in the larger world by building Christian hearth and home. In our parenting work, we are not simply nurturing children, but raising adults, who embrace the faith and serve the Lord in their own generation. Importantly, the battle for society takes place on the mantle of the family. <Callout>The personal heritage and blessing that is a godly family is also a redemptive blessing and spiritual influence to our fallen world.</Callout>

##### May See Your Children's Children: Hoping for Generational Impact

Godly parents who take their responsibility seriously not only seek to raise children fit for the fight of faith, they also seek to make a generational impact. In God's design, families extend beyond the life of their parents. If taken seriously, parents can establish a home that transfers godly values, biblical ideals, pious examples, and timeless truth into the next generation. These biblical texts say that "children are indeed a heritage from the Lord" (127:3). In fact, godly parents are given a vision of seeing their "children's children" (128:6). The blessing of seeing faithful followers of God in subsequent generations outshines any prospect of material wealth, public renown, vocational advancement, bountiful fare, or palatial estate. These psalms summon us to invest in our children, considering how our parenting work can impact generations to come. Moreover, they call us to invest in that which lasts—forsaking treasures on earth to store up treasures in heaven. Parenting our children in a godly way can be (and should be) an investment in these heavenly treasures.

These psalms serve like wisdom sayings. They offer us a reliable portrait for how things work in God's economy, but their do not guarantee these realities for every single parental experience. Tragically, not every godly parent will meet their grandchildren. Sadly, not every godly family will bear children. Not every parent who fears the Lord will have children who follow the Lord. Many times, God's promised blessings may not materialize this side of eternity. Nevertheless, this does not mean God's promises are not true and trustworthy. Instead, the portrait of a pious home with God's promised blessings will certainly come to fruition in the world to come, if they do not come or only come in part in this life. Eventually, the generations will end, and the Lord will return. Only then, in retrospect, will we be able to see how our invest in our families and children were summed up in the work of Christ. Only then, will we see how God's steering hand used our fragile and frail efforts to accomplish his purposes in the world. Only then, after many have forgotten our name, will we hear from the Lord who has written our names in the book of Life: well done my good and faithful servant.

##### Conclusion

God's wisdom in the psalms is rich and rewarding. It not only offers us a blueprint for a godly household order, it also captures our spiritual imagination for a vibrant family life. The work of homebuilding is God-honoring work. In many respects it is a central and vital work in our larger lifework. The work of faithful homebuilding flourishes when marked by the fear of God, first by parents and then be children. God has given promises of blessing for faithful families and dutiful parents. But faithful building of hearth and home is meant to have an impact beyond itself influencing society, contending for the faith, impacting generations to come, and investing in eternity. <Callout>Done according to God's design, few vocations like Christian homebuilding make a greater impact in the world.</Callout> To this end, may we consider our calling as parents noble and fashion our family around God's grand and eternal purposes.
