# Session Two

### Between the Ages: Nurturing Faith in the Next Generation

#### Overview

> Then we Your people, the sheep of Your pasture, will thank You forever; from generation to generation we will declare Your praise.

<< Psalm 79:13

> The power for good or evil that resides in a little child is great beyond all human calculation. A child rightly trained may be a world-wide blessing, with an influence reaching onward to eternal years. But a neglected or misdirected child may live to blight and blast mankind, and leave influences of evil which shall roll on in increasing volume till they plunge into the gulf of eternal perdition.

<< George Müller, *George Müller of Bristol*

God is accomplishing his work throughout the ages. In every generation, God has preserved his people. Across the epochs of human history, God works his mighty salvation. The story of the world is the grand narrative of God achieving merciful rescue and gracious redemption of people from all cultures and all times. One of the main vehicles that passes the faith from one generation to the next is the family. Godly homes are tributaries that nurture and nourish devotion to God in the lives of others. Good or bad, the influence of a family extends for generations. By nature, families are intergenerational. Children, adults, and the elderly coexist in mutually edifying ways. But the intergenerational dynamic of the family precedes the current generation (through the influence of one's ancestors) and extends to those yet born (marking the lives of one's descendants). Importantly, the church—as a family of faith—is also an intergenerational community. Each generation must look back to its forefathers with thanksgiving and caution as it consider what it means to live out the faith in the present age. When children are born, our focus should (rightly) turn toward the future, reflecting how our parental work can pass on the faith for generations to come. Such is the great and noble work of parenting: stewards dutifully passing on the faith of our fathers.

#### Key Idea

As stewards, we will pass on the faith of our fathers.

#### Introduction

> Hear this, O elders; and give ear, all who dwell in the land. Has anything like this ever happened in your days or in the days of your fathers? Tell it to your children; let your children tell it to their children, and their children to the next generation.

<< Joel 1:2–3

> The Christian home is a stewardship. The parents are stewards of God .... They are invested only with delegated authority. Their home is held by them only in trust. It belongs to them in the same sense in which a household belongs to a steward. It is not at their absolute disposal .... These children are the heritage of the Lord; they are not at the absolute disposal of their parents; but merely entrusted to their care to be educated and dealt with according to the will of God .... Thus the interest of the child should be the reward of the parent. God will, it is true, reward the faithful steward of the family .... As the faithful stewards of God, we should dedicate our household in all respects to Him, and make it tributary to His glory.

<< Samuel Phillips, *The Christian Home*

God calls parents to be primary caretakers of their children. As caretakers, parents must assume the role of a faithful steward—a servant who seeks to use their position, power, and influence to diligently oversee that which has been entrusted to their care. God has not only entrusted our children to us, but he also charged us with the responsibility of delivering the truth to our children. Fundamentally, the primary duty of stewards is to please the master, putting into order a household that ultimately belongs to God. As stewards, parents recognize God as the master of their home. As such, God's will serves as the charter for all family life. His authority operates as the governing rule of the household. His revealed truth is the creed and canon confessed. His revealed word is the blueprint and design for family order. God has delegated to parents the responsibility to raise their children in the truth and pass on the faith to the next generations. In this way, children belong more to the Lord than they belong to their parents. The stewardship of parenting vocation requires that fathers and mothers see themselves as a part of an intergenerational community of faith, nurturing devotion to God in a way that preserves and promotes the faith for future generations.

#### Biblical Wisdom

Grandchildren are the crown of the aged, and the glory of a son is his father.

<< Proverbs 17:6

##### Core Principle

Parenting takes place in the midst of the generations. We are recipients of the past and cultivators of the future. The intergenerational nature of the family (and the Christian faith) is a most precious gift. Within a home, it is considered a great blessing to see one's children's children. As the proverb says, "grandchildren are the crown of the aged." Godly parents who see their children raise children of their own recognize this as a Godgiven privilege not experienced by everyone. Grandchildren are honor, joy, and dignity to those advanced in years. As much as grandparents bask in the blessing of grandchildren, children find great honor and dignity in their parents. The proverb teaches that "the glory of a son is his father." Children find importance, worth, meaning, and significance from their parents. Instinctively, children look up to their parents as objects of affection, sources of security, and models of emulation. Godly families well-ordered after God's design enjoy the rich interaction, mutual dependence, and joyous celebration of the generations. When families dysfunction and decay, generations turn against each other to their dishonor and hurt. But godly parents foster the health of their home by relishing in the intergenerational nature of childrearing—past, present, and future.

#### Passing the Faith to the Next Generations

<< But You, O Lord, sit enthroned forever; Your renown endures to all generations.

<< Psalm 102:12

> Every father of a family should consider himself as charged with the souls of those whom he hopes to leave behind him, and as contributing to the future propagation of the truth, by every act of devotion performed in his house. Wherever he has a tent, God should have an altar.

<< James W. Alexander, *Thoughts on Family Worship*

##### Introduction

When we think about the nature of a family unit, we must marvel at God's creative genius. Through families we maintain a connection to the distant past and leave a legacy for the coming future. It is not uncommon for a family unit to have three or four generations living at the same time, experiencing this intergenerational reality is real time. The tight-knit bonds of intergenerational family relationships are powerful, shaping our values, beliefs, character, and worldview. Each individual person owes his existence to an extended ancestry of marriages and births that were ultimately out of his control. Families exist to bring other humans into the world that will eventually form families of their own. Only God knows how the impact our lives and families will make in the distant future. According to God's design, the family is a self-propagating organism—called to be fruitful, multiply, and fill the earth. The joy of marriage naturally gives way to the desire for children.

But marriage and family are meant for more than a mere procreation. Because our children embody the future, it matters what kind of people our children become. Children require nurture, care, and guidance. Left to themselves, children will be misshapen and malformed. <Callout>Parents are frontline workers in faith formation:</Callout> *nurturing devotion to God by passing the faith to the next generation*. Psalm 78:1–8 is a spectacular parenting psalm that captures this vision for parenting. While surrounding culture defies God's design for family, marriage, and parenting, it is incumbent upon Christian parents to restore God's vision for godly parenting: parents are stewards of God's timeless truth who pass on the faith of the fathers to their children by cultivating a family life of intergenerational relationship, Christian community, and devotion to God. One of the greatest ways to impact society is by faithfully attending to the raising of our children after God's design. Let's be attentive to how this psalm can help us understand parenting as most sacred duty before God, toward our children, and for the sake of the world.

##### Our Fathers Have Relayed to Us: Parents as Stewards of Divine Truth

God has appointed parents as stewards of the truth. Godly leadership in the home must involve depositing the content of the faith to their children. Psalm 78 draws attention to this crucial calling in the family and the faith community. God's people can only reach maturity if they establish their lives on divinely revealed truth. As God's revelation to mankind, the Bible contains "instruction" (78:1) to be heeded. Through the Holy Spirit, God's inspired word has been preserved by the "words" (78:1) of his faithful followers. Divine truth is not always easy to understand, but, when it is embraced with faith, God's words are like "parables" (78:2) that help elucidate the riddles and quandaries of life. These parables record the story of God's redemptive work in saving his people. The truth of God's word is an ancient and timeless truth, revealing "things hidden from the beginning" (78:2) that were previously concealed but have been progressively revealed over time. God's word not only contains "law" (78:5)—commanding us in the ways of life—but also bears "testimony" (78:5) to God—inviting us to put our faith and trust in the Lord. Ultimately, the truth of God's word bears witness to God: declaring "the praises of the Lord and His might" (78:4), celebrating "the wonders He has performed" (78:4), and recounting "His works" (78:7). <Callout>Parents are best caretakers of their children by being faithful custodians of the truth.</Callout>

Parents must not be mere defenders of the truth. They must faithfully deliver the truth to their children. It is important that parents and families have a vision and strategy for communicating God's truth to their children. Helpfully, Psalm 78 offers a mosaic of terms to describe the act of delivering the truth: "open mouth" (78:2), "utter" (78:2), "relayed" (78:3), "not hide" (78:4), "declare" (78:4), and "tell" (78:6). While the psalm addresses the responsibility of the teacher in faithfully delivering the truth, it also enjoins the recipients to humbly accept the truth: "give ear" (78:1), "listen" (78:1), "heard" (78:3), and "known" (78:3; cf. 78:6). Communication of the truth is a two-way street: faithful teachers who pass on the truth intact to the next generation and a younger generation that is humble, teachable, and receptive to accept the truth as divine revelation.

Christian parents are temporary caretakers of their children. God has lent children to us for a short time in order to raise them in the faith. While we will always enjoy a parental relationship with our children, this relationship will change over the years. The years when children live in our home are to be focused on faith formation. <Callout>The aim of parenting is to eventually release the next generation into the world, rooted in uncompromising godliness and fervent faith.</Callout> From a biblical standpoint, the stewardship of children involves being custodians of divine revelation. At its most basic level, Christian formation in the home is a matter of delivering and receiving: Christian parents bear responsibility to faithfully and earnestly deliver the truth their children, and children bear the responsibility to humbly and simply receive the truth in genuine faith.

Like church leaders, parents are spiritual leaders in their home. One of the most fundamental jobs of a "parent as pastor" in homebuilding is to communicate God's truth to the family. Biblical truth should be the canon by which all other truth claims are measured. The character of God should be the defining standard of what is good, right, and moral. The testimony of God's works should form the exuberant confession and collective memory of the household. The unchanging ancient truths of Scripture should be the sure guide to navigate ever modern complexity and conundrum. The redemptive storyline of Scripture should shape worldviews about the true story of the world. A home and hearth built on the truth—testimony about God's holy character, inestimable worth, and redemptive work—grows a family strong. Strong families make the community healthy. Healthy communities impact and influence surrounding society. Leading one's home in faith formation is one of the best ways to leave a mark in the world that lasts beyond one's limited life. faith.

##### To the Next Generation: Being Intentional about Intergenerationality

The work of faith formation is an intergenerational work. But as this psalm attests, the influence and impact of generations is multifaceted. In one sense, we are the recipients of the faith from the previous generation: the truth is that which "we have heard and known and our fathers have relayed to us" (78:3). As recipients of the faith, the current generation is responsible to deliver the faith to next generation: the truth we have received "we will not hide them from their children, but will declare to the next generation" (78:4). This responsibility is a moral and spiritual duty assigned from God: God "commanded our fathers to teach to their children" (78:5). Our intergenerational duty should not be onerous but hopeful. We should bear witness to God's word and works so that "the coming generation would know them" (78:6). Our vision of faith formation should include "even children yet to be born" (78:6). But the aim of faith formation is not simply to deposit the content of the faith, but the capacity for each generation to pass on the faith for themselves. Even more, this vision of international faith formation aims to impact the unborn generation so that they will "to arise and tell their own children" (78:6). But not everything from past generations is worthy of emulation. Inasmuch as we should receive the faithful testimony of truth passed down, we should also be warned by faithless examples from the past: to "not be like their fathers, a stubborn and rebellious generation" (78:8).

The life of faith is intrinsically intergenerational. So should parenting intentionally be. <Callout>The Christian family is the glad beneficiary of the faith from saints in prior generations.</Callout> Even if one cannot identify Christians in their own family tree, each believer is indebted to the interconnected chain of families and communities who preserved the faith from generation to generation. As a believer, faithful saints in the past are your heritage—your spiritual fathers and mothers—even if they are not in your immediate bloodline. As parents, we should strive to make our parenting as intergenerational a possible. We should celebrate the ancient faith, rehearse the lives of the saints of old, share testimony of God's past dealings with his people. We must warn about the mistakes of the past and forecast the challenges of the future. We must train our children to not only embrace the truth, but have the competency to pass on the truth to others. We must be ever cautious of the danger of forgetting God. We must build strong our collective memory: frequently retelling the true stories of God's dealings with his people. We must see children as a heritage from the Lord. In the raising of children, we are nurturing future adults, those who will have families of their own. We should reflect on how our parenting work can impact three, four, or five generations into the future.

And no parenting plan would ever be complete, without a concerted effort to incorporate the faith community as a mainstay in their family life and faith formation efforts.

He Established a Testimony:

##### Recentering the Role of the Faith Community

The transfer of truth to the next generation is a community venture. The appeal to faith formation in this psalm is clearly set within the covenant community—"give ear, O my people" (78:1). God established his revealed truth "in Jacob" and "in Israel" (78:5). Both of these phrases serve as references to the collective of God's people. Even the repeated household terms in this passage—fathers (78:3, 5, 8) and children (78:4–6)—depict the larger corporate body of God's people as a faith family investing in the homebuilding of each individual family unit. The household of faith has the building of healthy family units as one of its main tasks. Not only does the family play a vital role in intergenerational faith formation, but God's people function like a family, sharing in the work of passing the faith to the next generations.

<Callout>Christian hearth-building must be set within the context of the faith community.</Callout> While the sphere of the family is sacred and God-ordained, individual family units do not possess within themselves all that is needed for childrearing in the ways of God. Although each family should function like a "little church," it should not be a replacement for the church. Within the faith community, parents find plentiful resources for their parenting vocation: intentional mentoring, marital exemplars, spiritual oversight, biblical teaching, meaningful fellowship, personal accountability, corporate worship, moral development, doctrinal instruction, habit formation, selfless service, material aid, and missional purpose. None of this means the parents forgo their primary shepherding role and hand over the child raising responsibility to the community. Rather, it means that godly parenting earnestly fulfills its faith formation responsibility by setting its household firmly the life of Christian community. Christian community assists in the passing of the faith to the next generation. The faith community is a like a spiritual incubation chamber that nurtures families, marriages, and children to be spiritually mature.

##### That They Should Put Their Confidence in God: Nurturing Devotion to the Lord

<Callout>Faith formation in the home and in the faith community aims to nurture people in devotion to God.</Callout> This psalm makes explicit the purpose of our intergenerational and community-based parenting work: "that they should put their confidence in God, not forgetting His works, but keeping His commandments" (78:7). This purpose statement highlights a threefold goal for the next generation: genuine trust in God himself, vigilant memory of God's deeds, and faithful obedience to God's word. True devotion to God must involve receptivity of the truth and rejection of falsehood. The psalmist describes the goal of faith formation as forsaking the faithlessness of previous generations: to "not be like their fathers, a stubborn and rebellious generation, whose heart was not loyal, whose spirit was not faithful to God" (78:8). But avoiding falsehood and error is not enough. We need to nurture children who entrust themselves to God.

Christian homebuilding is a matter of nurturing Christ followers in devotion to God. When parents communicate God's word to their children, too often they merely seek a child's behavioral conformity to family rules. While respect of a parent's authority, order in the home, and obedience to God's commands are important, none of these represents the overarching goal of Christin parenting. Above all, Christian parents should seek for their children to put their confidence in God (cf. 78:7). <Callout>Genuine faith in God</Callout> will express itself in faith devotion. A parent ought to forgo all convenience, any ease, and every worldly benefit for their children to have wholehearted trust God. When this faith is born in the child, parents must nurture this faith: helping them to not forget God's works and to express their faith in obedience to God's commands.

##### Again and Again: Retelling the Sacred Story to Our Sons and Daughters

The rest of the psalm (78:9–72) narrates the true stories of God's dealings with his people. The author cyclically rehearses key portions of Israel's history, telling of God's marvelous works—"what He had done, the wonders He had shown them" (78:10). This record of God's dealings with his people across the generations recounts God's miraculous liberation from Egyptian slavery (78:12– 14, 42–53), God's supernatural provision during wilderness wanderings (78:15–16, 19–20, 23–31, 40–41), God's gracious enablement to take the promised land (78:54–55), and God's righteous anger (78:21–22, 31, 59) and just punishment (78:30, 33–34, 59–64) for his people's faithlessness. Broadly speaking, the history of Israel tells a narrative of faithlessness. Despite God's glorious works, God's people showed cowardice in battle (78:9), infidelity to the covenant (78:10, 57), disobedience to God's commands (78:10, 40, 56), forgetfulness of God's works (78:11, 42), brazen and perpetual sin (78:17, 32), rebellion against God (78:17), willful testing of God (78:18, 41, 56), inordinate craving (78:18, 29), doubt and unbelief (78:19, 22, 32), lack of reliance upon God (78:22), false repentance (78:37–38), grieving God (78:40), provoking God (78:41), and the vile practice of idolatry (78:57– 58). This heavy and harrowing list of faithlessness is the result when people do not place their confidence in God (78:7, 22). Generations can go awry without faith in God. But God remains faithful, calling his people out of sin and into genuine repentance (78:32–37), undeserved grace (7:38– 39), and divine victory (78:65–72). The psalm ends with the hope of God's promised Messiah coming to deliver his chosen people. God under the skillful hands and pure character of God's chosen deliverer (78:67–72).

<Callout>The redemptive story faithfully recorded in the Bible is the faith heritage of every believer.</Callout> While God's truth can be stated in propositions, much of the Bible is an unfolding narrative of God's gracious work to redeem his people. Not only should parents rehearse the story of their own particular family, but they are responsible to ground their children in the story of stories. God's word records the history of God's ancient people as our story. Those who place their faith in God become inheritors of God's work among his people. We should retell the true stories of God's dealings with his people, because it tells the true story of the world. The faithlessness of our fathers serve as a grave warning. The enduring truth must become our firm bedrock. The wondrous works must stir us to worship and praise. God's grace and forgiveness should encourage us to embrace a broken and contrite heart of genuine repentance. By retelling the story, we proactively build a faith memory in our homes that encourages confidence in God and hope in his redeeming grace.

On the lap of a mother civilizations have been laid, as she sings, teaches, and comforts her little ones. Culture is built by fathers instructing and teaching their children about God's wise word and wondrous works. Through fireside chats and long strolling walks, grandparents have forged societies by their storytelling of the days of old. Pastors, teachers, and elders have been world-builders, telling the next generation the things that have been since the beginning.

##### Conclusion

Few vocations are more challenging and rewarding than parenting. <Callout>When God forms a family, he invites us to cultivate a home.</Callout> Christian homebuilding requires us to properly and precisely view ourselves as custodians of truth—divine revelation of God's words and works that serve as the only sure foundation for meaningful existence and everlasting life. Establishing a home on the truth will require us to grapple with the intergenerational nature of parenting—grateful for past faith heritage, dutiful in forming the next generation, and hopeful for impact across many future generations. The intergenerational perspective of godly parenting helps us see the impor- tant role of the community of faith—an intergenerational context to incubate faithful followers of God. All this work aims at cultivating devotion to God. May God help us rise up and form the next generation in our homes and church communities with this grand, God-sized vision that can sustain us in the midst of daily responsibilities, encourage us when undergoing great difficulty, and embolden us to stand firm regardless of the uncertain challenges on the horizon for each generation.

#### Session Summary

- The goal of parenting is to pass the faith to the next generations: to faithfully deliver God's word and fervently recount his works to the next generation.
- Parenting is an intergenerational endeavor. We are influenced by the past generations. We should seek to parent to influence generations of those not yet born.
- Godly families help the church grow; healthy churches help families mature. The church is a family of families that seeks to establish homes in the faith.
- Godly parents nurture faith in God: to put their confidence in God and follow his commands.
- Passing the faith to the next generation involves sharing the history of God's saving work: a true story of redemption that culminates in the work of Christ on the cross.
