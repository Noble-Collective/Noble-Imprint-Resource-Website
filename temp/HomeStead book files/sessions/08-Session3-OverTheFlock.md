# Session Three

### Over the Flock: Tending to Our Children Like Shepherds

#### Overview

> I will raise up shepherds over them who will tend them, and they will no longer be afraid or dismayed, nor will any go missing, declares the Lord.

<< Jeremiah 23:4

> Christian parents! See and accept your blessed calling; you are the shepherds of the Divine love to tend and feed the lambs. In His Church the Chief Shepherd has many shepherds to care for the flock, but none who can so care for the lambs as the parents. 'He makes Him families like a flock:' it is to parental love, inspired and sanctified by redeeming love, that Jesus looks for the building up of His Church ... 'Feed My lambs:' when this word is made the law of a parent's duty, what gentleness and love will it inspire, what heavenly hope, what faithful, watchful care, and what an unceasing life of faith in the love and grace and blessing of Jesus on our home.

<< Andrew Murray, *The Children for Christ*

God has appointed parents over their children. But we must not wield parental authority merely for our own benefit, treating children like servants and slaves. Instead, we should be over our children like a good shepherd is over his beloved sheep. Christian parents are to view their home as God's flock entrusted to their care. There is great promise, inspiration, and practical help in viewing the parenting vocation as shepherding work. First, Christian parents are under-shepherds, caretakers who tend to their domestic flock as servants of the Good Shepherd, Jesus Christ. As such, Christian parents are sheep in God's flock before they are shepherds over their children. They learn to care for their children as they receive God's loving shepherding care. Second, shepherding work parallels duties of godly parents. Shepherds protect, guard, feed, and tend to the flock with utmost care. To embody the role of a shepherd in one's home is to display selfless love, put on Christlike humility, cultivate a watchful and vigilant spirit, and nourish their children in the love of God. As pastors tend the church as God's flock, Christian parents are to view their homes in a churchly fashion—as a flock that requires attentive and godly shepherding oversight.

#### Key Idea

As shepherds, we will lead our children as God shepherds us.

#### Introduction

Come near me, then, my God, though I am so feeble, and as a tender parent feeds his child, and the shepherd carries his lambs, so come near to me."

<< Charles Spurgeon, "Peter's Prayer"

God is the Great Shepherd of his people. Showing his benevolent care, God provides for the needs of those who belong to him. With divine might and unsurpassable strength, God protects his people from predator and foe. Leading his people on the paths of righteousness, God disciplines and corrects. God demonstrates his love for his people in his tender care and loving supply. God nurtures, guides, secures, defends, and oversees his people as a diligent shepherd watches over his flock—all out of love and concern. Christian parents must care for their children the way that God tends to his people. When godly parents view their home as a flock, the can start to learn how to shepherd their children the way that God has shepherded them. In fact, parents best shepherd the flock of their families when they entrust themselves to God's shepherding care. Parents fulfill their shepherding role best when they assume their role as under-shepherds: God's representative caretakers assigned with the task of guiding their children under the gracious supervision of God's shepherding care.

#### Biblical Wisdom

Be sure to know the state of your flocks, and pay close attention to your herds.

<< Proverbs 27:23

##### Core Principle

Parenting work is like the work of a shepherd. A faithful shepherd provides for his sheep, defends them against enemies, leads them on safe paths, and corrects them when they err. A good shepherd shows his care for his sheep out of great love and genuine concern. But a loving shepherd is best able to care for his sheep when he knows what they need. This proverb calls us to "be sure to know the state of your flocks" and "pay close attention to your herds." Shepherds must be attentive to their flocks. Good shepherding care depends upon the ability of the shepherd to accurately and regularly know the condition of one's sheep. Likewise, parents must carefully attend to their children: understanding their needs, watching for danger, scoping the environment, evaluating their health, nurturing their growth, and admonishing them toward godliness. Parenting as shepherds requires this first great work: paying close attention to surroundings, knowing the needs of one's children, and discerning our family culture.

#### Shepherding Work in the Family Flock

> Know that the Lord is God. It is He who made us, and we are His; we are His people, and the sheep of His pasture.

<< Psalm 100:3

> Act lovingly to your children. In all your counsels and commands let them read love. Love will command honour; and how can a parent but love the child who is his living picture, nay, part of himself. The child is the father in the second edition.

<< Thomas Watson, *The Ten Commandments*

##### Introduction

Perhaps no context more shapes a person than the family setting. Family life is intensely formative. It is in the home that young children learn about faith, morality, relationships, values, work ethic, and life habits. During our earliest years of life, we are shaped by conversation, hobbies, humor, and shared experience in the home. The values upheld on the hearth eventually trickle into mainstream society. The influence of the family can go in two directions: leading children on the paths of righteousness or misguiding them on the road to destruction. <Callout>We often indirectly "pick up" as much from our family culture as we do from direct instruction.</Callout> Consequently, how we parent our children is as important as what we parent the in. The mode of parenting is as crucial as the content of our teaching and substance of our discipline.

Although many metaphors helpfully describe a parent's role and calling, one dominant biblical metaphor stands out to capture a godly mode of parenting. Throughout the Scripture, leaders are commonly referred to as shepherds—overseers who care for God's people like a herdsman tends to sheep. This term is used of Israel's rulers and church leaders. Even God himself describes his relationship to his people with shepherding terms. For this reason, we turn to Psalm 23:1–6 to aid us in our parental journey of Christian hearth-building and homebuilding. This psalm will help us learn basics of good shepherding in hopes to apply these skills in the way we care for our children. Ultimately, we hope to shepherd our children like God shepherds his people. Certainly, parents are not a replacement for God, but they are set up in their parental role as representatives of God. Because God relates to us as a shepherd, we should apprentice ourselves to his divine care. In the Lord as "my shepherd" we will surely find no better example.

##### The Lord Is My Shepherd: Exploring God's Shepherding Work

God rules with divine strength, righteous character, and loving concern. He is unrivaled in the way he cares for his own. God's people express their deep appreciation for and childlike trust in God's care with the words: "the Lord is my shepherd" (23:1). The shepherd image is quite vivid, depicting a vocation that involves oversight, care, guidance, and consistent supervision. God tends to his people as a shepherd watches over his flock—alert, attentive, tender, circumspect, and vigilant. Sheep depend upon the shepherd for their livelihood and safety. Shepherds feed, protect, lead, and defend their sheep, even at the cost of their own life. God as shepherd and believers as sheep are both fitting descriptions. What is noteworthy in the opening confession of this well-known psalm is the claim that God is "my" shepherd. This is a claim to personal relationship with the overseeing and caregiving God. God's shepherding care extends across his flock but is also tailored to the individual needs of each member of his covenant community. As a shepherd knows his sheep and sheep know their shepherd, so God is personally and relationally involved in caring for his people.

<Callout>Parenting work done in a shepherding mode emulates God himself—the Shepherd of shepherds.</Callout> Sheep are needy, weak, and vulnerable. Left to themselves they wander, stray, and get into danger. Without a shepherd to defend them, sheep are easy targets to predators. But sheep are most treasured by a loving shepherd. Parents need to be vigilant shepherds of their children, studying the way God cares for his own people. In parenting children is the work of providing for the needy, caring for the weak, defending the vulnerable, and guarding the flock from harmful predators. But godly parents must not only learn to shepherd as God shepherds; they first must be shepherded by God. In fact, a parent's ability to shepherd their children is directly related to their own submission to and receptivity of God's shepherding care in their own lives. We ever remain more fundamentally God's sheep than God's under-shepherding servants. Said another way, parents shepherd their children best when they are submissive sheep under God's faithful shepherding hand.

##### **Green Pastures and Quiet Waters: Enjoying God's Plentiful Provision and Guidance**

The opening of this psalm describes God as a shepherd who provides for his people. God's kindness and tender care are highlighted by the way he brings his people to "green pastures" and "quiet waters" (23:2). In these places, sheep can safely find food and drink. Green pastures bears witness to the fact that God supplies sufficiently for his people. Quiet waters attests to God sustaining his people in rest and repose. The result of God's provision is that the sheep "shall not want" (23:1). All their needs are met by the benevolent and open-handed shepherd. God holistically cares for his sheep —body and soul. In the psalm, God's shepherding care results in a refreshed and renewed inner being. God's provision causes the sheep to testify: "He restores my soul" (23:3). <Callout>God nurtures and uplifts, feeds and encourages, and nourishes and rejuvenates. He sustains body and fortifies soul.</Callout> But God's provision is more than kindhearted holistic supply, it is omniscient personalized care. God knows everything that his sheep really need and he is involved in their lives. God is the sovereign and unerring guide and director of the sheep's movements. The sheep testifies about the Lord: "He makes me lie down" (23:2), "He leads me" (23:2), and "He guides me" (23:3). God is able to amply and abundantly meet the needs of the sheep because he is an all-knowing and all-wise shepherd. Ultimately, the physical provisions for the sheep culminate into moral guidance: God guides his people "in the paths of righteousness for the sake of His name" (23:3). God's shepherding care expresses itself by leading his people to the best of all goods: God himself.

God appoints parents over the care of their children. Like a shepherd, parents are in a position to know more about the world and life than their children. From birth, infants are like sheep, utterly reliant upon their Godappointed caretakers. It can feel draining and depleting to constantly meet the needs of our children. The joy of parental work is providing for the needs of precious little humans who can offer us nothing in return. In this way, parenting demands a selfless spirit of constant sacrifice and outpouring generosity. Like faithful shepherds, loving parents put the needs of their children before their own comfort, convenience, or ease. But faithful parenting that lovingly and holistically meets the needs of our children is an honorable and joyful work. Christian homebuilding involves providing a place where needs are met—physical, emotional, relational, and spiritual. A home with parents who embrace shepherding care will communicate God's unmatched omnibenevolence by their thousand little acts of daily provision. As Christian hearth and home become a place of loving provision, they also become a place of nurture and formation—a place where trust is built, dependence is rewarded, faith is established, security is experienced, and goodness is embraced. Certainly, parents need to decipher the difference between a child's needs and wants. Parents want to give good things to their children, but they need wisdom so that their parental provision does not spoil the child. Nevertheless, parents always seek to offer their children what is best. In the end, this means guiding our children on a path where they can experience God's shepherding care for themselves.

##### Your Rod and Staff Comfort Me: Receiving God's Caring Protection and Correction

God's provision for his people includes his protection over them. God's loving supply is matched by his loyal strength. God is no weak overseer; he is an unmatched shepherd-warrior. God watches over his sheep with a guarding eye and mighty hand. He guards them from dangers without and threats within. He protects his sheep from enemies, and disciplines his sheep away from dangerous paths. The psalmist testifies about God's defending and discipling care: "Your rod and Your staff, they comfort me" (23:4). The rod is a weapon used to ward off predators; the staff is an instrument of guidance, correction, and leading. Together, these two shepherding tools represent God's protection and discipline. <Callout>Comfort is found in God's safeguarding rod and his corrective staff.</Callout> God uses his power to protect his people from enemies and to guide his people on the right path. Divine power applied in this way results in comfort. Sheep take refuge in God's power to defend them against foe and fiend, adversary and opponent. But sheep also come to love God's merciful correction as much as his mighty protection, because they know his powerfully guiding and corrective hand is keeping them from eventual danger.

Parental work is a balanced work of protection and correction. God-appointed shepherds over their home, parents safeguard their children from evil, defend against evildoers, shelter them in difficulty, and guard them in a violent and vicious world. Equally, parental work involves correction and discipline: warning about immorality, admonishing against bad relationships, rebuking falsehood, and confronting dangerous and deadly living. Protection and correction are are not antithetical to each other. To protect a vulnerable child is to demonstrate love and care. To correct an errant child is equally an expression of love and care. The rod in one hand and staff in the other is a faithful way to raise children. Godly discipline is a form of parental protection; parental protection can prove the loving intent of godly discipline. Certainly, parents can be overprotective—coddling children to the point where they face no difficulty or danger and leaves them unprepared for life. But parents can also forsake their protective duty, offering up children to a vulturine culture and society. To shepherd as God shepherds us, we must bear both rod and staff toward comforting ends.

##### You Are With Me: Experiencing God's Peace-Giving Presence and Nearness

Divine protection does not mean that God keeps his people from all danger and difficulty. This psalm assumes that sheep will find themselves in harrowing circumstances. When sheep enter difficulty and danger, God's presence is their enduring peace. Even when God's sheep "walk through the valley of the shadow of death" (23:4), they can enjoy rest and repose. The constant refrain of the God's people is a confession of trust in God's immanent presence: "You are with me" (23:4). The presence of God overpowers all menacing threats, allowing God's people to bear witness: "I will fear no evil" (23:4). Protection does not mean avoiding all difficulty, but seeing how God provides in the midst of surrounding enemies. In fact, God's people testify that God will "prepare a table before me in the presence of my enemies" (23:5).

As God's presence offers peace to his people, parental presence is a mainstay for their home. <Callout>Our faithful presence with our children can be one of our greatest ministry modes to our children.</Callout> Children thrive by healthy attachment and bonds with their parents. Healthy bonds with parents in the younger years can best prepare children more their more independent release into the world. As children build their trust in parents who faithfully shepherd them, the hope is that the children are learning to trust ultimately in God. There is a distorted version of reliance upon parents—a codependence in which parents care for their children for their own sense of self-worth, self-esteem, and selfsignificance. In this malformed view of parenting, parents care for their children for the benefit it brings back to themselves as parents. If not careful, this unbiblical mode of parenting can create entitlement, spoil character, and stifle faith. But there is also an unhealthy absenteeism in parenting, where parents are not involved and not present with their children in meaningful and healthy ways. Sometimes parents hand over the responsibility of parenting to others in the community, forsaking their primary duty to shepherd their children. No person is in a better spot to influence their children than the parents. We must avoid self-serving codependence and irresponsible absenteeism.

Much is at stake in our parenting venture. <Callout>In many ways, parenting amounts to preparing our children to live faithfully in a dangerous world a world rife with dark valleys, deadly shadows, evil forces, and hostile enemies.</Callout> Faithful presence with our children helps them learn how to face difficulty with hope in God. As shepherds, we walk with our children in painful valleys so that they are prepared to endure suffering for themselves. Daily, we commune in the presence of God so that our children can learn to trust God and find victory over death itself. As a family, we live faithfully in a sinful world—embracing countercultural values in our home, teaching our children how to walk with God, and showing them through our example how to fear no evil. We feast on God's bountiful provision while enemies surround, adversaries malign, foes ridicule, and opponents intimidate us. All the while, we do not flinch at their threats. We prepare our children for this dangerous and deadly world by entrusting them to God's shepherding care.

##### I Will Dwell in the House of the Lord Forever: Looking Ahead to God's Future Promise

God shepherds his people toward future promise. The end of the psalm expresses confidence that God's people will eat a banquet even though their enemies surround them (23:5). In this banquet, God's people enjoy his blessing, signified by the phrase, "You anoint my head with oil" (23:5). The blessings God provides for his people are promised to be abundant: "my cup overflows" (23:5). But God's banquet feast is more than physical food and drink (cf. 23:1). God promises that he will bountifully supply for his people, giving them a continuous supply of unending good: "surely goodness and mercy will follow me all the days of my life" (23:6). But the our shepherding Lord promises even more: eternal fellowship with God for all time. The climactic conclusion of this psalm bears witness to this promised reality of eternal life with God: "I will dwell in the house of the Lord forever" (23:6). <Callout>A faithful shepherd, God leads his people securely from this life into the world to come.</Callout>

Godly parenting is grounded upon God's promises. Faithful parent-shepherds orient their children to God's blessing, training their appetites to long for all that is truly good, beautiful, and right. Certainly, some of God's promises are experienced during out brief earthly lives. In response to these provisions, God's people offer up thanks and praise to God. But some of God's promises will only come to fruition in the future: eternal life, unending fellowship with God, final defeat of enemies, removal of sin and unrighteousness, and defeat of the shadow of death. When parents discharge their parental duty as faithful shepherds, they calibrate their family culture to God's good and trustworthy promises. As Christians, a healthy family culture will yield the fruit of godly virtue when it longs for God's blessing and promises above all else. Christian homebuilding involves celebration of God's faithfulness, thanksgiving for timely provision, praise for undeserved blessing, expectation for bountiful supply, and hope for full life of unmixed good and world without end. No better parenting plan exists than to build heart and home on the foundation of divine promise and unmerited blessing.

##### Conclusion

Godly parents follow God's example as a faithful and loving shepherd. But godly parents first recognize that their shepherding capacity to give care to their children is directly dependent upon them accepting God's shepherding care in their own lives. We can only give to others what we first receive from God. As faith-filled sheep we faithfully shepherd our children. In this way, God's shepherding example becomes a most helpful and holy paradigm for parenting. In our work of provision, our vocation of leading, our duty of protection, and our ministry of presence, may be emulate the strong and kind shepherding care we have experienced from God in our our own lives.

#### Session Summary

- Parents are primary shepherds of their children and should care for their children the way God cares for his people.

- As shepherds, parents should provide, guide, protect, and correct their children with love. As shepherds are present with their flocks, so parents should minister to their children with faithful presence.
- Parents shepherd their children by leading them into God's promises—certain hope of a blessed future.
- Parents shepherd best when they receive God's shepherd care in their own lives.

## Session 3 Reflective Projects

### Personal Project

*The following activity helps you internalize the biblical teaching and develop theological wisdom for parenting by grappling with the implications of the teaching in your own life.*

#### Project Instructions

Consider how parenting is shepherding work. Write a job description for parenting that describes it in shepherding language. Consider the challenges that will be faced, the skills that will be needed, the motivations that will be essential, and the outcomes that will be hoped for.

#### Example Job Description

- As a shepherd, I am tasked to lead and guide my children. I will steer them to the timeless truth of God's word and to the paths of righteous living.
- As a shepherd, I am called to feed and nourish my family. I will study God's word so that I can teach and nurture my family on your eternal revelation. I will provide for my children in body and spirit, keeping them from unhealthy teaching and falsehood.
- As a shepherd, I am called to protect and guard my family. I will be a sentry to my home, being watchful of what is happening in surrounding culture. I will not let evil and wickedness infiltrate our walls or ears or hearts. I will be vigilant and wise with technology, cautious to not unleash the digital world prematurely upon my children, teaching them how to use it wisely, and modeling this in my own life.
- As a shepherd, I will minister and care for my family with my faithful presence. I will be around the family and involved in their lives. I will study each of my children personally, so that I know their heart and they know my voice.
- As a shepherd, I will place our home under the safekeeping of Christ—the Good Shepherd. I will teach them that the safest and most blessed life is to be one of God's flock, fully entrusted to Jesus' care.

#### Family Values

In this project, you will list out your family values. First, work through the categories to help describe your family. Then, summarize your convictions into a summative statement (i.e., bullet points, creed-like statement, lyrics, etc.). The aim is identify values that will define the culture of your home in a summary form. Consider the following categories to evaluate current values and envision more God-centered values:

- **Current Evaluation**

- What We Give Most of Our Time To Now (Time, Money, Discussion Topics, etc.)
- How People Might Describe Our Home Now

- **Future Aspirations**

- The Characteristics We Want to Define Our Home
- The Things We Want to Be Most Important to Us
- **Family Creed**: Summary of Biblical Values to Define Our Family
