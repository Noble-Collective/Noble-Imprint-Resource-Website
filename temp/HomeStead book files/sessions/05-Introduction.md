### No Longer Two: Receiving Children as Gifts from God

#### Overview

> "I prayed for this boy, and since the Lord has granted me what I asked of Him, I now dedicate the boy to the Lord. For as long as he lives, he is dedicated to the Lord." So they worshiped the Lord there.

<< 1 Samuel 1:27–28

Are you solicitous that their bodies may be cloath'd: you should be more solicitous, that their souls may not be naked, or go without the garments of righteousness.

<< Cotton Mather, *A Family Well-Ordered*

This opening session serves as an orientation for a rite of passage into Christian homebuilding. Specifically, this rite of passage celebrates and marks the journey of raising children and building a home in a way that honors and glorifies God. No longer two (just husband and wife), you are welcoming children into your family. The stakes are high. The investment made in a child's life in its early years can profoundly shape them. But the joys are greater than the challenges. Few things are more rewarding than to see children grow in godliness, embrace the truth, take ownership of their faith, worship the Lord, and serve God's purposes in the world. In this introductory session, you will preview the overall structure of this process and explore the underlying rationale for such a rite of passage. Additionally, you will study a core passage from the life of Jesus that highlights this milestone in his earthly life. Parents, spiritual mentors, trusted peers, church leaders, and the wider church community are all integral to this journey into parenting. These key people are invited to this introductory session so that they can support and encourage you as you gain a theological vision for parenting your family in life with God. The starting point to this great and noble work is the realization that our families belong first and foremost to the Lord.

#### Key Idea

Our home belongs to the Lord.

#### Introduction

You are no longer just two. At this season of life, you are welcoming children into your family. This is God's design. It's is a beautiful blessing and a weighty responsibility. You likely feel both of these competing realities simultaneously. At times, you are stricken by the gift of your child (or children), grateful for the life of this child intricately fashioned in the image of God. Other times you are overwhelmed by the new duties and countless activities involved in nurturing your little ones. As God allows, the blessing of marriage eventual turns into the blessing of parenting. According to God's design, a husband and wife committed to each other in the covenant of marriage are called to be fruitful, bear children, and establish a family. Sometimes a family is established through adoption and fostering of other children. In all cases, healthy families are foundational to society. Healthy families throughout the world are best grown when they have a vision for being a godly family before the Lord. Rather than merely wandering into this parenting role, this study will seek to mark this milestone in your life in a way that helps you think about your parenting vocation from a biblical and God-honoring perspective. Godly parenting begins with consecrating our family and children to the Lord. In fact, it begins with fully dedicating ourselves, as parents, to God. In this session, we will overview the process of this rite of passage and discuss a biblical foundation for what we will experience in the weeks to come.

#### Biblical Wisdom

The righteous man walks with integrity; blessed are his children after him.

<< Proverbs 20:7

##### Core Principle

This biblical proverb gives us insight into the nature of Christian parenting. It teaches us that one of the main means of parenting is a parent's faith maturity and Christian character. The person who "walks with integrity" is considered "righteous." Essentially, a life fully consecrated to God results in a transformation of one's character. Those who live a life devoted to God can hope for God's favor on their children: "blessed are his children after him." This rite of passage process offers a pathway for you to mark your entrance into parenting: to order your home after God's design, to establish your home on Christ, to cultivate a home that enjoys God's blessing, and to make a plan to raise your children in the ways of God. This study is an invitation to Christian homebuilding, beginning with the sobering reality that our home and heart usually take the shape of our own character. Let's seek the Lord in the our homebuilding endeavor by taking a look at the landscape of our own lives.

#### Dedicating Hearth and Home to God

> Then the Lord said to Moses, "Consecrate to Me every firstborn male. The firstborn from every womb among the Israelites belongs to Me, both of man and beast."

<< Exodus 13:1–2

> The business done in the home is nothing less than the shaping of the bodies and souls of humanity. The family is the factory that manufactures mankind.

<< G. K. Chesterton, "New Witness"

##### Introduction

Bearing children is a major milestone in life and faith. But *childbearing* is not the same as *childrearing*. The gift of each child born into the world is soon followed by a sobering sense of duty and responsibility to raise each child in a God-honoring way. All parents are accountable before God for how they raise their children. The weighty responsibility of this godly duty is balanced by the worthiness of the work. During the child-raising journey, parents experience deep joy and painful difficulty, they anticipate hopeful dreams and fearful uncertainties, and they feel initial excitement and eventual exhaustion. In our families, in the raising of our children, and in the cultivation of holy homes, we are enlisted in a great and glorious endeavor. <Callout>Through the family, society is shaped, the community is formed, the church is established, and the generations build God's kingdom.</Callout>

God has not left us without guidance in the sacred work of building Christian hearth and home. As with every other area of life, Christian parents look to Scripture to align their families and shape their parenting vocation after God's inspired plan. In Luke 2:21–40, Jesus is dedicated to God: the promised and eternal Son of God is dedicated to God the Father. As God-honoring parents, Mary and Joseph modeled faithful devotion and parenting foundations. They treasure baby Jesus most by offering him back to his heavenly Father. Not only does their example help us consider the work of parenting in general, but it calls us to the very specific duty of establishing our families—our marriages, our homes, our children—on the person and work of Christ. Every child is a gift and blessing from God, a gift best kept secure in God's safekeeping care. But Jesus is a special gift from God: God's one and only Son given to mankind for salvation. There is no better starting point to the parenting journey than to consider how to consecrate one's family and child to God, by laying a family foundation of Jesus Christ—the eternal Son of God who came into the world as a human baby. Let's look to Mary and Joseph as paradigmatic examples of this most noble task of building hearth and home devoted to the Lord.

##### Consecrated to the Lord: Establishing a Family Devoted to God

Soon after Jesus' birth, Mary and Joseph performed several faithful acts which dedicated the infant Christ to God: *circumcision*, *naming*, *presentation*, and *sacrifice*. As a Jewish practice, Jesus' circumcision established him within the Israelite covenant community. Naming Jesus was both an act of obedience—heeding the angelic message by giving him "the name the angel had given Him before He had been conceived" (2:21)—and expectation—anticipating Jesus' work of salvation. The journey to Jerusalem to "present Him to the Lord" (2:22) was a law-abiding act which "consecrated [Jesus] to the Lord" (2:23). The sacrificial offering of "a pair of turtledoves or two young pigeons" (2:23) took place "when the time of purification according to the Law of Moses was complete" (2:22; cf. Leviticus 12:1–8). This worshipful act signified atonement, cleansing, and removal of impurity. Emphatic in this biblical narrative is the devotion of Marry and Joseph to live "according to the Law" (2:22; cf. 2:21, 23–24, 27, 39). For the holy family, personal faith, marital priorities, and family life were aligned and submitted to God's word.

Although we are not bound by Jewish law, the faithfulness of Mary and Joseph provides an inspiring example to families welcoming children into the world. <Callout>Our very first acts as Christian parents should not be child-centric but God-focused.</Callout> That is, to mark one's home by worship, to consecrate our children to the Lord, to elevate the need for purification and atonement, to embed oneself in the community of faith, and to honor God's word as authoritative are fundamental foundations for all faithful families. Joseph and Mary tended to baby Jesus as stewards of a gracious gift given them by God. They realized that they could only best care for their child when they viewed that child as first belonging to God. Their firstborn son Jesus was first and foremost God's eternal only begotten Son. God had entrusted him to their temporary care. A family devotes itself to the Lord as it realizes that each and every child is a gift from God. And we raise our children as temporary caretakers of that which ultimately belongs to God.

The godliness of Joseph and Mary were paramount in shaping their home culture. Importantly, the godliness of one's marriage sets the tone for family culture and values. <Callout>Holiness and intimacy enjoyed between husband and wife irrigate the garden of the home.</Callout> But the vibrancy of a Christian marriage rests upon the individual devotion of each spouse to the Lord. As leader of the home, the father's piety is pacesetting—families most often go the way of the dad. The mother's godliness fosters a fertile environment for faith formation. The godliness of a home, including the maturity of husband and wife, is sustained by the church and contributes to the well-being of the Christian community. In reality, *child dedication* is a matter of *family consecration*: surrendering one's family wholly to God before the people of God. This wholehearted devotion takes place across several vectors: the child devoted to God, parents committing themselves to raise the child in the faith, and the church community committing themselves to the family and the childrearing process. Whether one has children or not, to be devoted to the Lord is to be dedicated to help all the children in the body of believers grow to full maturity. Families and churches mutually edifying each other in devotion to the Lord is the sweet symphony of being a part of the people of God.

##### Into the Temple Courts: Raising a Family Rooted in Faith Community

Even though Jesus was born when Mary was a virgin, both Mary and Joseph raised Jesus as his parents (2:33). The collaborative nature of parenting, between husband and wife, is reinforced by the loving support of a covenant community. Jesus' dedication in the temple (2:27, 37)—the gathering place of worship for God's people—signifies the important role of community in homebuilding. It was in temple that Jesus' parents met two godly individuals: Simeon and Anna. Simeon was "righteous and devout" (2:25). His life was surrendered to the Holy Spirit: "the Holy Spirit was upon him" (2:25), "the Holy Spirit had revealed to him" (2:26), and he was "led by the Spirit ... into the temple courts" (2:27). Anna lived a life characterized by spiritual service: she was "a prophetess" (2:36). Statements about her marriage (2:36), widowhood (2:37), and constant presence in the temple (2:37) highlight her godly character and uncompromising devotion to God. Her faith formation and maturity is emphasized by a myriad of spiritual practices: worship, fasting, prayer, and gratitude (2:37–38). Jesus' presentation in the temple, in the context of aged and godly saints, represents the important role the faith community plays in raising godly children.

No parent was meant to raise children alone. Not only is parenting the teamwork of husband and wife, but it is also the responsibility of the faith community. Children raised in a Christian church community mature as they are surrounded by older believers who take their faith seriously, practice spiritual habits, cling to God's promises, rehearse God's redemptive work, and and build their lives on God's saving promises. Seasoned believers in the faith community, like Simeon and Anna, can become a support to parents as they seek to raise their children in the discipline and instruction of the Lord. Godly examples of faithful believers who live a life worth emulating is one of the most powerful tools of faith formation.

<Callout>For all believers, to be devoted to God is to be dedicated to God's people. One cannot exist without the other.</Callout> There is a mutual interdependence that exists between families and churches. As a family of families, church maturity can often be measured by the health of its household units. Conversely, family maturity is reliant upon a strong faith community. The busyness of family life, the exhaustion of daily (and nightly) parenting duties, the difficulty of troubleshooting household dilemmas can easily keep us from the church. Many have foregone church fellowship in the name of caring for their families (cf. Hebrews 10:25). What a woeful and foolish decision! It is precisely in the church community that a household can blossom into a family of faith. For it is in the church where the larger purposes of God are worked out in his world and where God's people—including parents and children—get regularly acquainted with God's great and glorious promises to his people.

##### As You Have Promised: Fastening a Family Secure to God's Word

Parenting in community is fueled by divine promises. Jesus' dedication in the temple was more than just about the supportive influence of faith community. It was about the God who works to save his people and form the community of faith. Inasmuch as Simeon and Anna represent godly Christian community, they more fundamentally signify the hope of salvation. Simeon was "waiting for the consolation of Israel" (2:25) and Anna was "waiting for the redemption of Jerusalem" (2:38). Consolation and redemption are brought about by God's "salvation" (2:30), made possible by "the Lord's Christ" (2:26). The message of salvation—in fact, the culmination of all God's promises—is good news revealed about Jesus Christ (2:33, 36), the only one who save people from sin, judgment, and condemnation. This salvation God had "prepared in the sight of all people" (2:31). God made promises and came through on his promises so that salvation could become available for all people (2:31–32) who embrace Christ. All centers on Christ. He is the one who will reveal the thoughts of mankind (2:35). Jesus is the center upon which all people—individuals, families and communities—rise and fall (2:34).

In this narrative, obedience according to the Law (cf. 2:21–24, 27, 39) is buoyed up by faith that God does "as [he has] promised" (2:29). Christian homebuilding is an intentional setting of one's family on the rock of God's unalterable promises. And all of God's good promises are fulfilled in Christ. In fact, this narrative reminds all Christian homes are built on Christ. First, Simeon "blessed God" (2:29) for the Christ—an act of worship—which turned into a blessing of the holy family: "then Simeon blessed them" (2:34). The blessing of the home was connected to a heartfelt praise of God for the salvation that he had brought to fruition in Jesus. Consequently, Christian homebuilding must establish a gospel-centered, Christ-focused, salvation-oriented foundation. This is what child dedication, homebuilding, and family order is all about.

Parenting's main goal is for children to embrace Christ and experience his salvation. Homebuilding begins by a commitment to establish a household on Christ. In this story, Christ is more than an example of child dedication. He is the means by which salvation can come to our household. <Callout>A child dedicated in each Christian home must ultimately be devoted to the Christ child dedicated in Jerusalem.</Callout> When a home is dedicated to Christ, the family can bask in divine comfort, enduring peace, worship-filled awe, and everlasting joy. The first steps in ordering a home is submitting the home to Jesus as Savior and Lord.

##### And the Child Grew: Cultivating a Family as Context for Spiritual Growth

All child dedication and homebuilding rests on the hopeful gospel message announced during Christ's own dedication in the temple. Parenting is difficult and challenging work. Sometimes the parental duty can feel burdensome and overwhelming. Sometimes it can cause pain and heartache. This biblical text even testifies that Mary would experience pain as Jesus' mother: "a sword will pierce your soul as well" (2:35). Divine promises sustain us in the work. But God's promises in the gospel are not just for the life to come, but realities we can experience in this present life as well. In this way, the difficulty and labor of parenting should give birth to spiritual fruit and Christian maturity. In this narrative, Jesus' godly and faithful parents provided a rich context for faith formation (2:39). No doubt, their worship in the Jerusalem temple continued as faithful family formation in "their own town of Nazareth" (2:39). Over the course of time, Jesus "grew and became strong," he was "filled with wisdom," and "the grace of God was upon Him" (2:40). In fact, we learn that Jesus grew holistically across various domains of life (2:52).

Parents hope for growth in their children like the development Jesus experienced. When the gospel is the foundation of a home it provides a fertile context for spiritual formation. Godly parents hope for growth, strength, wisdom, and divine grace. To teach the gospel without instructing in godliness is a great fault of unbiblical parenting. Christian parents build their homes on Christ when their preaching of the gospel of Christ translates into instruction in the ways of Christ. Salvation is not possible outside of Jesus. If parents miss this simple point, all their efforts are vain and worthless. But no better pattern for godly living exists than Christ himself. Christ himself should be the curriculum of our home. Inasmuch as parents must keep the gospel at the center of their home, they must embody, exemplify, and commend Christlikeness as a formation goal. <Callout>Building home is like planting and cultivating a garden where seeds of infants and sprout, grow, blossom, and bear fruit for righteousness.</Callout>

Helpful metrics of godliness are outlined in Jesus' own development and formation. Although Jesus was fully God, he was also fully human (without sin). He experienced growth and maturity common to mankind. It is important to notice the holistic dimension of Jesus' growth. Over the course of his infancy, childhood, and youth, Jesus experienced physical, intellectual, moral, social, and spiritual development (2:40; cf. 2:52). This record of Jesus' development provides a helpful blueprint for Christian parents. With Jesus at the faith foundation of our families, we should uphold Jesus as the ultimate example by which we should shape our lives. Jesus is the means of salvation and the model of godliness. Our parenting should facilitate formation in all areas of life: establish faith in Christ, cultivate intimacy with God, promote holy relationships, instruct in biblical wisdom, foster physical well-being, encourage spiritual discipline, and stimulate moral living. But lest we think we discharge our parenting duty in human strength, we should remember that the key to the godly culture in the household of Joseph and Mary should be the key to our homes as well. The definitive mark of Jesus' life was that he was under the canopy of God's gracious favor. If we hold any hope for our families to be mature in godliness and our children to be courageous saints, it will be by the grace and favor of God. Let us pray that our children rest under the supernatural safety and enabling power of God's grace.

##### Conclusion

Jesus' dedication in the temple teaches us about parenting as much as it also recounts the gospel. Certainly, Christ's birth and dedication announce the good news of salvation to a lost and sinful world. But the events of this biblical narrative also help parents order their home and establish their family with Christ at the center. <Callout>Christian homebuilding is a matter of establishing our homes on the foundation of Christ.</Callout> Child dedication signifies that a home is serious about submitting their whole family to Jesus: the Christ child dedicated in the temple in Jerusalem. In sum, a Christian home is dedicated to God, rooted in Christian community, established on God's promises, and well-ordered to maximize spiritual growth. Taken seriously, these simple yet profound truths will help a family welcome its first children into the world (and into their family) in a way that seeks the child's best. And the child's best is a life fully offered to the Lord.

#### Session Summary

- Jesus's parents were fully devoted to the Lord and consecrated Jesus to God with wholehearted commitment and aligned their life and family according to God's word.
- The godliness of parents directly affects the godliness of the home. Godly parents make sure their individual walk with the Lord and their marriage is growing in Christlikeness.
- Godly families are rooted in Christian community because the community of God's people provides a rich context for spiritual growth through faith practices, godly examples, and corporate worship.
- Christian families build their homes on God's promises which are ultimately fulfilled in Christ. The good news of salvation in the gospel must serve as the foundation and focus of all parenting work.

#### Mentoring and Community Roles

This rite of passage is best completed in the context of close relationships. Crossing into a new season of life is best facilitated with a network of trusted fellow travelers. In a Christian setting, these relationships can come from family, friendships, and the church community. *Church leaders* or *heads of household* help guide and direct this process, usually occupying the main teaching role. Going through this rite of passage with a group of *peers* can reinforce the ideas and provide a supportive community into the future. *Mentors* help encourage the participants along the way, helping them reflect on learnings, providing a structure for accountability, and promoting internalization of key biblical teachings. Usually, mentors have gone through the rite of passage or have already passed into the next season of life and are equipped to help encourage participants in their journey. The *family* and *church community* can help bolster the experience through orchestrating the experience, seeking to maximize the holistic and experiential essence of the rite of passage. Family and church are to be the main environment in which this rite of passage is worked out. *Couples* are encouraged to work through sessions individually and collectively. Here are some steps to make this rite of passage a rich and full-orbed experience.

- Identity trusted *church leaders* to teach and lead the main sessions.
- Pair up participants with *mentors*. Have mentors touch base with participants throughout the experience. Prepare mentors for their task throughout the main study sessions (Part One) and the mentoring sessions during the outdoor challenge experience (Part Two).
- Gather a group of *peers* who are going through the same experience together. While this rite of passage can be done individually, it will be an enriched experience with a community of peers.
- Marshall the extended *family network* and *church community* to help encourage, organize, and facilitate this experience. These groups can also help prepare for the time of celebration.
