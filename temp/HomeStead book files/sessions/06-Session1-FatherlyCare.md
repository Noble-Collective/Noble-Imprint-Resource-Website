# Session One

### Under God's Fatherly Care: Building a Home Devoted to God

#### Overview

> And when the days of feasting were over, Job would send for his children to purify them, rising early in the morning to offer burnt offerings for all of them. For Job thought, "Perhaps my children have sinned and cursed God in their hearts." This was Job's regular practice.

<< Job 1:5

> We may take great comfort from considering that, God stands to us in the relation of a Father ... He must from His very nature do everything in the best and most perfect manner, and in the fullest also; we may be sure He will be to us better than ever any earthly father has been to a son .... We have a Father in heaven; and He will not come short in the fulfilment of even one of the functions of a parent .... And because He is a Father you may expect all tender comfort.

<< Philip Bennet Power, *The Sick Man's Comfort Book*

The first session of this study explores the source of all godly parenting: God himself. Just like our Christian devotion to God is based upon God's unfailing commitment to us, so our parenting work is fueled, sustained, ordered, and empowered by our vibrant relationship with God as Father. To know and trust God as Father—to experience his divine compassion and tender love—is essential if we are to care for our children in the right way. After all, the whole aim of our parenting work is to initiate our children into their own relationship with God. Spiritual practices, household habits, and family worship all serve to establish our children in the faith to take ownership of their spiritual life, enjoying their own walk with the Lord. Our job as human parents is to encourage a relationship with God as Divine Parent. In this way, parents are like priests in their homes. Their sacred calling is to nurture children in the worship of God, a *most holy work* that begins with us fostering a *most humble walk* with the Lord.

#### Key Idea

As priests, we will nurture our children in God's fatherly care.

#### Introduction

> At that time Jesus declared, "I praise You, Father, Lord of heaven and earth, because You have hidden these things from the wise and learned, and revealed them to little children. Yes, Father, for this was well-pleasing in Your sight."

<< Matthew 11:25–26

> It is a sweet compound of faith that knows God to be my Father, love that loves him as my Father, joy that rejoices in him as my Father, fear that trembles to disobey him because he is my Father and a confident affection and trustfulness that relies upon him, and casts itself wholly upon him, because it knows by the infallible witness of the Holy Spirit, that Jehovah, the God of earth and heaven, is the Father of my heart.

<< Charles Spurgeon, "The Fatherhood of God"

God is a loving and caring Father to his children. As divine parent, God is not merely the exemplar of loving parenting work, he is its eternal source that energizes and enables godly parenting. We can only love our children toward God as we cultivate and sustain a vibrant relationship with God as Father. The childlike faith that defines our trust in our heavenly Father is the foundation and fuel for parenting our children in a godly way. Parents are God-appointed intermediaries—like priests—leading their home in the worship of God. Certainly, parenting work is epitomized by God the Father. But God is more than a parenting paradigm; God himself is the power, strength, and vitality each parent needs to be faithful in this most important work of rearing children. Godly parents can only build Christian hearth and home with the divine aid and loving presence of God the Father.

#### Biblical Wisdom

Trust in the Lord with all your heart, and lean not on your own understanding; in all your ways acknowledge Him, and He will make your paths straight.

<< Proverbs 3:5–6

##### Core Principle

The foundation of all parenting is our relationship with the Lord. Few things like the responsibility of childrearing can make us confront our limits, weakness, and inabilities. The invitation to parenting is a similar invitation to the life of faith, to "trust in the Lord with all your heart." Faith in God requires us to heed a second instruction: "lean not on your own understanding." Trust in God means we must divest trust in ourselves. However intelligent or educated a person may be, the work of childrearing will expose the unreliability of our own wisdom and the inadequacy of our skills. Parenting requires trust in God—a humble confidence in God in our efforts to build Christian hearth and home. God backs his instruction with good promises. If we fully depend upon God—"in all your ways acknowledge Him"—there is a hope that he will direct our steps—"He will make your paths straight." We are not alone in the parenting work. Not only because we have a faith community, but because we have a loving heavenly Father who is desirous to direct the every step of those who trust in him.

##### Introduction

In this first session, we explore this fundamental principle: each parent's relationship with God as Father is hugely significant in sustaining, shaping, and energizing all parenting work. <Callout>Relationship with God best outfits us for our parenting vocation.</Callout> To know and enjoy God as loving Father is to equip ourselves to build Christian hearth and home. In Psalm 103:1–22, the Bible gives us a compelling picture of God's divine parental care toward his people. This biblical hymn of praise explicitly attests to and celebrates the fact that God treats his people like a loving father. Throughout the psalm, the author fleshes out specifics of how God cares for his people, inviting us to establish our lives—and parenting—in God's fatherly love. No father or mother can expect to care for their children—or build a godly home—without being sheltered, sustained, and supported by God's divine love for his people. It is under the coverage of divine compassion that our parental labors have divine power.

Christian homebuilding is an expression of a life of worship and devotion to God that is animated by God's fatherly love, sustained by God's divine care, and is established upon God's covenantal faithfulness. Where worship, devotion, piety, faith, and obedience have priority, childraising takes on a Christian character. If worship is the sturdy foundation of every healthy Christian home, it requires us as parents to cultivate our life with God. The business done in the home—which has a most important influence upon society—has its genesis in our walk with and worship of God. We neglect this foundation to our great peril and embrace it to our enduring joy!

##### All That Is Within Me: Being Captured by a Vision of God's Glorious Rule

The dominant refrain in Psalm 103 is "bless the Lord" (103:1; cf. 103:2, 20–22), an invitation to praise God for his character and work. This psalm—an uninhibited hymn of praise—extols God for his "holy name" (103:1) and his "kind deeds" (103:2). When the psalmist cries "O my soul" (103:1; cf. 103:2, 22), he shows us that genuine praise should come from the depths of our being—sincere adoration exclaimed from our inner person. God-honoring worship is also an act and attitude of wholehearted devotion, offering to God "all that is within me" (103:1). Such undivided resignation to the Lord shapes our focus and attention, making us careful to "not forget" (103:1; cf. 103:18) divine kindness in its many-sided array. The psalm tells us that God deserves worship from "all His angels mighty in strength" (103:20), "all His hosts" (103:21), and "all His works in all places" (103:22). God is worthy to be worshiped by all creation—animate and inanimate.

This psalm makes it clear that worship of God invigorates a life of piety and obedience. Genuine worship is more than verbal homage and energetic song. Those who truly worship God are his "servants" (103:21) who "carry out His word" (103:20), "hearken to the voice of His command" (103:20), and "do His will" (103:21). God's people are marked as "those who keep His covenant" (103:18), and those who "remember to obey His precepts" (103:18). The life of obedience comes from an underlying faith in God, made possible because God's people "fear Him" (103:11; cf. 103:13, 17). The fear of God moves God's people to submission, expressing their devotion as those who live under "His dominion" (103:22).

Every godly parent's hope and desire should be to establish their home on the foundation of worship. Worshiping families are marked by exuberant praise of God's worth, godly fear of God's majesty, humble obedience to God's commands, and willing submission under God's rule. Parents best lead their homes by regularly acknowledging God as King. Parental authority is only a derivative authority (an authority given by God) that seeks to use its own position and influence to direct its household under the safety of God's benevolent rule and reign. A godly home recognizes that "the Lord has established His throne in heaven, and His kingdom rules over all" (103:19) and earnestly seeks to make its household under this same benevolent rule.

<Callout>Parenting work is buoyed by a life of vibrant worship to the Lord.</Callout> This most basic and vital aspect of the Christian life—doxology—is the foundation of all godly parenting. To the extent our walk with the Lord is vibrant and sincere, our parenting vocation will be focused and fervent. Our lateral, human-to-human relationships with our children will only be as formative and meaningful as our vertical relationship is with the Lord.

Elevation of God's majesty is the best starting point for parental fidelity in the home. The home is a place to experience and recount God's peerless character and powerful deeds. <Callout>Childrearing is not ultimately a matter of deference to parental authority, but of fostering devotion to God.</Callout> A child's responsibility to honor and obey their parents is a means to inculcate a reverence to the ultimate authority, God himself. To raise children Godward is to raise children who will trust God in faith and serve him in obedience. Hearth-building and homebuilding are a matter of cultivating a family culture of worship, devotion, obedience, fear of God, and submission to God. To build our home in this way, in the end, is to have our home placed under God's merciful and compassionate dominion.

##### As a Father Has Compassion: Resting in the Fountain of God's Fatherly Love

In great detail, this psalm celebrates God's love for his people. God's love is compared to the love a father has toward his children: "as a father has compassion on his children, so the Lord has compassion on those who fear Him" (103:13). God's fatherly care for his people is best described as "loving devotion" (103:4; cf. 103:8, 11, 17)—a love that pledges itself to the well-being of another in covenant faithfulness. God's covenant love expresses itself in unwavering commitment and unconditional fidelity. It is love beyond emotion and sentiment. It is care galvanized by divine promise, divine trustworthiness, and divine relationship.

God's multifaceted love toward his people is further explained in this psalm. Showing tender care for his people, God's love is "compassionate" (103:8; cf. 103:13). Exhibiting redeeming kindness, God's love is "gracious" (103:8). Showing patience, God is "slow to anger" (103:8), showing mercy to repentant sinners (103:9–10). Emphasizing the depth of his divine care and the faithfulness to his people, the psalm testifies that God is "abounding in loving devotion" (103:8). God's love is wide, separating us from our sins "as far as the east is from the west" (103:12). God's love is immense: "as high as the heavens are above the earth, so great is His loving devotion for those who fear Him" (103:11). The immensity of God's love corresponds to its unending and eternal nature: "from everlasting to everlasting the loving devotion of the Lord extends to those who fear Him" (103:17).

Love is the heartbeat of parents toward their children. Although human parents cannot perfectly replicate divine love, <Callout>God's compassion for his people is the great pattern and power of all parental love.</Callout> When our love for our children takes the shape of God's paternal love for us, we can remain committed to our children regardless of the circumstances. We can discipline with faithfulness, clarity, and grace. We can provide what is best for our children without catering toward their unsanctified desires. We can call our children to self-control, self-restraint, and self-denial as an act of formation and not mere punishment. We can display self-sacrifice even when our children do not deserve it. Our love for our children will only be "right-sized" when it is God-shaped.

##### Crowned with Loving Devotion: Following the Pattern of God's Tender Care

God's fatherly and covenantal love is expressed in unparalleled divine care. The body of this psalm elaborates upon God's "kind deeds" (103:2) to his people (103:7). In his mercy, God "forgives all your iniquities" (103:3). His disciplining anger turns into divine pity for those who trust in him (103:9–10). More than just ignoring our sin, God has graciously "removed our transgressions from us" (103:12). In his power, he "heals all your diseases" (103:3). In his grace, he "redeems your life from the Pit" (103:4). In his covenant loyalty and fatherly care, he "crowns you with loving devotion and compassion" (103:4). In his benevolence and provision, God "satisfies you with good things, so that your youth is renewed like the eagle's" (103:5). In his moral perfection, he "executes righteousness and justice for all the oppressed" (103:6).

Parental care for their children should take the shape of God's love for his people. Parental authority that is domineering and manipulative is misshaped and malformed. Parents should express their love for their children in the way that God cares for his people. Exemplifying God's own loving devotion, godly parents display kindness, extend mercy, practice forgiveness, and show grace. Christian homes should be places of healing and little communities where divine redemption is enjoyed. Parents who experience God's loving devotion to his people can extend this to their progeny. Christian homebuilding should bask in God's good gifts with gratitude and foster an environment where compassion is amply shown. Christian families should cultivate biblical values of righteousness and justice, caring for the oppressed. In the end, collective parental care toward children is the expression of God's divine kindness that each parent has individually received.

God's covenant-keeping love is also gentle and sympathetic to his people. He knows that people are not like himself. God is fully aware of our finitude and limitations. The psalm tells us that God "knows our frame" and is "mindful that we are dust" (103:14). Certainly, humanity is noble and beautiful and "blooms like a flower of the field" (103:15). But the intrinsic dignity and splendor of humanity is tem- pered by its fleeting and passing life: "as for man, his days are like grass" (103:15) and after a short time "the wind passes over, it vanishes, and its place remembers it no more" (103:16). The grandeur that is every human life comes to an earthly end, usually sooner than we want. God not only tenderly cares for us in our limited and weak estate, but he extends over his people a covenant love that will never end: "from everlasting to everlasting the loving devotion of the Lord extends to those who fear Him" (103:17). God's fatherly love cares for his people in the fragility of the present life and for unending life to come.

<Callout>God's care for us is our model and means to care for our children.</Callout> Parents must be fully aware of children's weakness, limitations, and frailty. What a parent knows from years of life experience, a child has yet to understand. Child-raising requires patience, a God-given longsuffering to come alongside smaller and immature human beings to help them grow and mature. Children are resilient, but they are also fragile. The early years of a child's life are often most formative and shaping, impacting personhood, character, and values for many years to come. Modeled after God's own parental care for his people, a godly parent seeks for the home to be a garden, cultivated for each child to bloom and blossom into all that God created them to be. But most importantly, parental vocation must tend to the eternal nature of each and every child. Godly parents celebrate the dignity of each child, helping them to bloom into all that created them to be. But homebuilding that simply attempts to maximize the "bloom" of this temporary life amounts to parental neglect. The parent's greater task is to help each child find coverage under God's eternal loving devotion, a love that secures eternal life and unending relationship with God.

##### Those Who Keep His Covenant: Enjoying Relationship within God's Faithful Promises

If the parent's primary duty is to bring their children under God's everlasting covenant love, then they must learn how to guide their children into relationship with God. God establishes his relationship with his people in a covenant (103:18). In this covenant, God promises faithfulness to his people and summons his people to covenant faithfulness by trusting God and following his commands (103:17–18). This passage calls people to fear of God (103:11, 13, 17). This fear is a way of describing genuine faith: a deep reverence toward God and abiding trust in God that results in awe of and attachment to God. Those who have sincere faith in God will express their faith in obedience (103:18, 20–21). <Callout>Homebuilding in piety encouraging genuine faith in and earnest obedience to God—can have a generational impact</Callout> where God extends his righteousness "to their children's children" (103:17).

Godly parents are gospel-centered, seeing their primary earthly vocation as fostering their children's relationship with God. There is no guarantee that parental faithfulness will translate into faith for each and every child. Ultimately, genuine faith is the work of God in the heart of each and every person. Nevertheless, a vision of parenting should foundationally be one that cultivates faith and obedience—the fear of God which leads to heeding God's commanding word.

But faith does more than merely enable obedience. Faith in God fosters attachment to God. Entrusting oneself to God initiates us into a covenant relationship with God. Our relationship with the Lord is secure, guarded and protected by God's good promises and faithful character. As we experience intimacy and security in our relationship with the Lord, so we are able to cultivate a home environment that fosters healthy forms of bonding, attachment, and connection. <Callout>It is in the home where children first learn what healthy relationships entail.</Callout> By keeping God's covenant front and center in our home, we can even hope for faith formation in the generations to come.

##### Conclusion

Christian homebuilding is a matter of cultivating vibrant worship of the Lord, encountering God's tender love, experiencing God's compassionate care, and enjoying God's covenant faithfulness. Cultivating Christian hearth and home is a matter of receiving God's grace and mercy in our lives. Our homes and hearts become welcoming and hospitable to others once we experience the extraordinary and unparalleled divine welcome of God's forgiveness, mercy, and grace. When parents maintain a life and faith of vibrant worship, their homebuilding endeavors benefit from God's blessing. Ultimately, this is the main aim of all parenting work: loving our children toward the love of God. As parents, may our devotion to God build a home that is under God's benevolent rule. May our experience of God's love and goodness fuel us to love our children with compassion, mercy, and faithfulness. May we build a home that experiences God's righteous care for generations to come, even to our children's children!

#### Session Summary

- God is the ultimate example of parental love and care. We learn best about parenting from the way God loves and cares for his people.
- Our relationship with God as Father shapes our own parenting work. We can only give to our children what we receive from God.
- Christian parents are like priests, nurturing their family in the worship of God. A heart of devotion to God is fostered by habits and practices of regular family worship.
- Godly parenting seeks to place their home under God's covenant love, fully submitted to God's righteous rule. Christian homebuilding cultivates devotion to God: genuine faith that leads to covenantal obedience.
- To be in covenant relationship with God is to experience an attachment to God. Parents should develop healthy attachments and bonds with their children to help them learn what it is to enjoy an intimate and close relationship with God.
- The Christian home is a place to teach gospel truths: redemption, mercy, forgiveness, and grace. Ultimately, the parent-child relationship is to introduce children to relationship with God—not to maximize earthly life's temporary and fleeting pleasures, but find coverage in God's everlasting love.

#### Project Explanation

##### Weekly Habits for Family Worship

In this project, you and your spouse should sketch out a typical week for your family. Identify the weekly rhythms that will help your family become a home of worship and devotion to the Lord. Consider fixed times for family dinner, family worship, and family recreation. Identify the specific habits and practices you want to incorporate in your family, considering the uniqueness of the morning, afternoon, and evening hours. Factor into your plan times of collective family worship, private devotion, and regular church fellowship.

List of basic spiritual habits:

- Bible Reading and Memorization
- Prayer and Fasting
- Sabbath Rest and Church Fellowship
- Service and Evangelism
- Generosity and Giving
- Praise and Worship

##### Family Worship Liturgy

Design a family worship session that you can use to lead your house in a time of praising the Lord. Look through the basic elements of a time of worship and seek to incorporate them into a liturgy—a planned and ordered time of worship. Consider how to make this time interactive with the members of your family, encouraging as much participation as possible.

List of basic elements of family worship:

- Scripture Reading
- Memorization
- Singing
- Prayer (Thanks, Praise, Confession, Petition)
- Catechism/Confession
- Encouragement
