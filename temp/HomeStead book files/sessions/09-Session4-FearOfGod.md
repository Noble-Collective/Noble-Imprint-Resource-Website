# Session Four

### In the Fear of God: Instructing Children in Godliness

#### Overview

> Listen, my son, and receive my words, and the years of your life will be many. I will guide you in the way of wisdom; I will lead you on straight paths.

<< Proverbs 4:10–11

> First, then, if you would train your children rightly, train them in the way they should go, and not in the way that they would .... If, then, you would deal wisely with your child, you must not leave him to the guidance of his own will. Think for him, judge for him, act for him, just as you would for one weak and blind; but for pity's sake, give him not up to his own wayward tastes and inclinations. It must not be his likings and wishes that are consulted. He knows not yet what is good for his mind and soul, any more than what is good for his body. You do not let him decide what he shall eat, and what he shall drink, and how he shall be clothed. Be consistent, and deal with his mind in like manner. Train him in the way that is scriptural and right, and not in the way that he fancies.

<< J. C. Ryle, *How Should a Child Be Trained?*

In this session, we explore the role of parent as teacher. Infants are born into the world without a well-formed understanding. They need to be taught. Children do not inherently perceive danger, grasp truth, or walk in righteousness. They need instruction, guidance, teaching, and direction. Youthful naiveté is initially inevitable and proves the need for education. Parental education is more than transfer of information; it is instruction in wisdom, discernment, and prudence. To gain wisdom is to gain skill in life, navigating the complexities of the world with God-honoring orientation. To educate in wisdom is to teach children what is true, good, beautiful, and right—leading them in a path of moral formation and training them to love what is holy. At its core, character development is rooted in the fear of the Lord. If we could summarize the underlying aim of all parenting work, it is this most simple yet demanding task: *instruct children in the fear of the Lord*. When deeply rooted in godly fear, children can grow up to be wise in the face of difficulty, godly when surrounded by evil, just in the midst of injustice, and faithful when experiencing suffering.

#### Key Idea

As teachers, we will instruct our household in the fear of the Lord.

The ultimate aim of parenting is to nurture children in the fear of the Lord. Only with this most vital foundation in genuine faith, children can experience true moral formation. When the fear of God is sown in the heart of the child, the core character of the youth undergoes a radical and dynamic change. They become new creatures in the Lord, orienting their conduct, speech, thoughts, desires, intentions, and relationships to what pleases God. When children grow in Christian character, their change of life is more than external modification of behavior, a change which does not weather difficulty or hardship. Genuine growth in godly character—that is, the cultivation of Christian virtue—is a substantive and lasting change. Those who fear the Lord can endure hardship, experience loss, face persecution, undergo disappointment, and suffer unmentionable ordeals with an abiding trust in God. What better aim of parenting can exist: *to raise children who relinquish their autonomy, revere God, relish his goodness, rest in his promises, and reform their life based on what pleases God*. To build Christian hearth and home in the fear of the Lord—a fear that rests upon tasting the unalloyed goodness of God—is to raise children as agents of transformation in the surrounding world.

#### Biblical Wisdom

Train up a child in the way he should go, and when he is old he will not depart from it.

<< Proverbs 22:6

##### Core Principle

The parenting vocation is a teaching work. God appoints all parents to be primary teachers of their children. The proverb pointedly commands us, "train up a child in the way he should go." The nuance to this instruction is important. First, training requires more than periodic lectures or verbal warnings. The best training is truth put into practice in daily life and modeled by the worthy teacher. Second, parents are to train their children in the "way he should go." There is a way that children want to go. The natural bent of children is to sin, selfishness, and destruction. The way he should go is a path of faith, self-surrender, and righteousness. Parents are the primary agents of education for their children. While there are many topics of education that are important to explore, a parent's most important work is to introduce children to faith in God. This proverb holds a promise along with its command: "when he is old he will not depart from it." This promise is not an absolute guarantee of a child's outcome. Many children receive godly training and walk away from the faith. Ultimately, children are responsible to apply parental teaching to their lives. Nevertheless, this proverb's promise summarizes a general truth: raise children in God's ways will have a lasting effect on their lives.

#### Forming Faith in the Fear of the Lord

> The fear of the Lord leads to life, that one may rest content, without visitation from harm.

<< Proverbs 19:23

> No man more truly loves God than he that is most fearful to offend Him ... Lord, who can know you and not love you, know you and not fear you; fear you for your justice and love you for your mercy; yes, fear you for your mercy and love you for your justice, for you are infinitely good in both.

<< Thomas Adams, *Works*

##### Introduction

As we seek to build our home on biblical and God-honoring foundations, we realize that homes are more than brick and mortar. The household that occupies the house is far more important is our work of Christian homebuilding. This means that our work of parenting involves focused work on our children growing into godly men and women. <Callout>At its core, parenting is a work of faith development and moral formation.</Callout> What we are seeking is much different than mere management of a child's external conduct. We are seeking spiritual change at the level of character and virtue. As we will learn, at the foundation of lasting spiritual transformation is the fear of the Lord. Consequently, we turn to Psalm 34:1–22, a passage invites children to come and learn the fear of the Lord. When the fear of God is at the foundation of a home, we can cultivate an environment where our children can come to experience and embrace God for themselves in genuine faith. Only as this happens, can we hope our children to be formed in godly character, a righteous character that comes from love of the glory of God, delight in the goodness of God, experience of the grace of God, and hope in the promise of God.

**At All Times:**

**Building Worship Habits to Live in God's Presence**

The psalm opens with a call to worship. The terms in this passage highlight what a life of worship entails: blessing (34:1), praising (34:1), boasting (34:2), rejoicing (34:2), magnifying (34:3), and exalting (34:3). Specifically, this psalm offers a call to continual doxology: unceasing worship of God "at all times" and "always be on my lips" (34:1). It is also a call for corporate worship—to praise God "with me" and to exalt God "together" (34:1). To continually and corporately worship God is to find remedy to life's challenges. This is why the humble, lowly, poor, needy, afraid, and distressed are invited to worship God (34:2, 4, 6). Communing in God's presence with a spirit of praise and a heart of worship is treatment to life's troubles.

This psalm's introduction is yet another reminder that worship is at the heart of the Christian life and should be at the foundation of all homebuilding. When a family consecrates itself to the Lord, it sets itself apart for one all-consuming purpose: exalting in the glory and goodness of God. Fathers and mothers will steward their parenting role best to the extent they prioritize the collective life of worship in their family. The appeal for continual worship in this psalm is instructing. Certainly, it is a call to develop habits of worship. <Callout>Parents serve their family by cultivating habits of worship early on in the lives of their children.</Callout> But designated times of family worship are not the only ways to cultivate habits of worship. Families can express God's praise all the time. During the thousand menial tasks and unending daily routines, we can let the praise of God "always be on my lips" (34:1). We Over time, the worship of God in ordinary and special moments builds a consciousness of God, teaching our children how to continually live in the presence of God.

##### At All Times: Building Worship Habits to Live in God’s Presence

##### Come, Children, Listen to Me: Calling Our Children to the Fear of God

A life of worship is fueled by the fear of God. At the center of this psalm and at the center of the Christian life—is a healthy and holy fear of the Lord. The psalmist describes what happens to "those who fear Him" (34:7; cf. 34:9), enjoins the saints to "fear the Lord" (34:9), and summons children to be instructed in "the fear of the Lord" (34:11). Importantly, this summons is given to children: "Come, children, listen to me; I will teach you the fear of the Lord" (34:11). The fear of the Lord is a synonym for genuine faith. When people genuinely fear God, they demonstrate faith in God by seeking him (34:4, 10), looking to him (34:5), calling out to him (34:6), and take refuge in him (34:8). Genuine faith as fear of the Lord results in divine aid (34:4), deliverance (34:4, 7), radiant joy (34:5), freedom from shame (34:5), gracious salvation (34:6), protection (34:7), blessing (34:8), and bountiful provision (34:9–10). Amazingly, when we have a fear of God, we can be delivered from all our other fears (34:4). Far from a fear that repels, the fear of the Lord is that which attracts and draws us toward the Lord. Those who fear God are those who "taste and see that the Lord is good" (34:9). They honor God in his holiness, revere God in his majesty, take God seriously for judgment, and cherish God for his goodness.

The thematic repetition in this passage not only helps us pinpoint a major biblical theme or a vital element of Christian living, but also helps focus our parenting work on a most essential foundation. At its core, the parental vocation teaches children the fear of the Lord—a summary of biblical faith. The fear of God involves awe of God's beauty, esteem of God's glory, terror at God's wrath, submission to God's authority, and trust in God's goodness. From infancy and childhood to adolescence and young adulthood, parents are key in helping children navigate their fears—learning to discern what is appropriate fear and fending off irrational and baseless fears. Healthy and holy fear of God will vanquish every other lesser fear. It will make a life whole and sturdy, fit to endure great difficulty without abandoning loyalty to God.

So much of life is determined by what we fear. And learning to fear God rightly is of utmost importance in our home. But faith-building by teaching the fear of God is not fear-mongering manipulation or authoritarian intimidation. Inasmuch as the fear of the Lord deters us from evil and alerts us to the dangers of self-centeredness, godly fear also attracts us to majesty of God, fostering awe, attachment, and allegiance that are constitutive of faith. To fear God is to bow before his might and take refuge in his power. To be drawn to God in fear is to be still and know that he is sovereign God, a truth which can calm inner fears and anxious hearts. To revere God's glory is to stand in amazement at what he has done and surrender ourselves to his service. To be terrified at his wrath is to come and receive his grace to be rescued from the path of condemnation. To treat the holy God as the one and only and in a category by himself is also to love and adore him as our God. Different than all the other fears that plague us, a healthy and holy fear of God draws us in to relationship with God.

<Callout>Godly parenting in the fear of the Lord teaches children to relish the Lord as much as revere him.</Callout> Not accidentally does this psalm put the fear of the Lord alongside a personal experience of God's goodness. Faithful parenting aims to help children "taste and see that the Lord is good" (34:9). Parental work seeks to help children to unquestionably know for themselves (through experience of personal faith) that God is good. In fact, parental work as a whole is training a child's appetites for what is truly God. A faith-building curriculum in the fear of God is a appetite-forming endeavor to train desires for what is truly good. to When parents teach their children the fear of God, they introduce them to a fear that fosters heartfelt worship, strengthens childlike faith and dependence, shapes desires, results in divine blessing, and triumphs over all other fears. A faith defined by such holy fear cannot but result in life transformation and spiritual growth.

##### The Man Who Delights in Life: Aiming for Deep Transformation in Character Formation

Biblical fear of God produces godly character and conduct. According to the psalm, those who fear the Lord "keep [their] tongue from evil" (34:13), "turn away from evil" (34:14), "do good" (34:14), and "seek peace and pursue it" (34:13). Those who embody godliness in life are those who have tasted the goodness of the Lord (34:8). Therefore, the person satisfied in the Lord practices righteous living as a person who "delights in life" (34:12). Those who reject evil and do good are those who have a desire for "good days" (34:12). Faithful followers are called "righteous" (34:15) in contrast with the wicked who "do evil" (34:16). In fact, those who fear the Lord experience God's divine favor: "the eyes of the Lord are on the righteous, and His ears are inclined to their cry" (34:15). <Callout>Genuine faith in God that is expressed in a holy fear of God results in deep, dynamic, and enduring change in life and character.</Callout>

> Parenting work is a matter of moral formation. This does not mean that parental work is reduced to mere behavioral modification. While godly parenting

upholds God's commands as the code of their household, moral formation is more than mere compliance to ethical commands. Yet focusing on good behavior, well-mannered politeness, and social courteousness is where most parents remain. Although parents should attend to a child's behavior—external conduct—the parental work aims at a much deeper change—godly character. When speaking of God's people, this psalm characterizes them as godly in speech, action, character, desire, and purpose. As a summary, those who fear the Lord in genuine faith are called righteous—mirroring the character of God. The character of their lives is fundamentally oriented to God that it has taken on a God-shaped form. Christian homebuilding is a matter of faith formation toward a transformation in godliness—a change at the level of one's person, being, and very self. Children who modify their behavior only to placate parental nagging usually do not sustain their adjusted conduct when the pressures of the world mounts. Instead, they quickly conform to the patterns of the world. But those who have a fundamental change in character—due to a deep and abiding faith in and fear of God—maintain their godliness in the face of temptation, sin, injustice, cultural pressure, and targeted suffering.

Parenting toward character formation requires parents themselves to embody the character they hope to inculcate in their children. By far, the most powerful tool in teaching is imitation. Students learn best by modeling themselves after their teachers. This is true for the development of skills and it is especially true in the area of virtue formation. No better place than the home is there for children to grow in character. In their parents, there should be a model of godliness worthy of emulation. This is where parenting becomes one of God's greatest instruments of sanctification. Our children are often mirrors of our own selves—reflecting back to us our gifts and our flaws. Once we recognize the importance of character formation and the power of our individual example, our children also become a motivation for us to take seriously our own formation in godliness. Our children will watch our speech, conduct, time management, relationships, and attitudes and often take after us. They will observe how we endure difficulty and learn to face hardship in ways we have modeled to them. Rather than shirking this duty as a teacher, let us embrace the fact that God has intertwined our own personal faith formation with the character development of our children.

##### The Afflictions of the Righteous: Raising Children Who Can Be Righteous Sufferers

Christian parents are called to train their children to maintain faith in God in a godless and hostile world. While the wicked face judgment, receive punishment, and experience just condemnation (34:21), the righteous will be delivered (34:22). The promise of ultimate deliverance for the righteous does not mean that they will not presently suffer. In fact, the ending of this psalm is a faithful testimony of the "afflictions of the righteous" (34:19). Biblical fear of the Lord draws God's faithful followers to deeper and more intimate trust in the Lord, knowing that they will encounter difficulty as they follow the Lord. Those who fear God rightly will cling to him desperately. When difficulties rise "the righteous cry out" (34:17) to the Lord for help. God's people oppressed by "all their troubles" (34:17) look to the Lord for deliverance. The "brokenhearted" (34:18) seek God's comfort and the "contrite in spirit" (34:18) plead for God's rescue. In the end, the fear of the Lord causes the righteous to "take refuge in Him" (34:22). The righteous sufferer knows that God hears his cry (34:17), provides complete deliverance (34:17, 19), offers his comforting presnce (34:18), secures salvation (34:18), protects his own (34:20) and redeems his devoted servants (34:22).

Popular and cultural parenting seeks to mitigate suffering in the life of their children. While Christian parents do bear a responsibility to protect and guard their families, godly households are places that prepare children to suffer well and maintain faith in God. During the work of Christian homebuilding, parents must seriously warn about the plight of the wicked, summoning children to fear of the Lord expressed in genuine faith. Genuine faith warns children about the path of destruction if they nurse a casual, dismissive, or irreverent attitude to God. But parents must also give an account of the righteous suffering—an honest portrait that God's people will endure hardship. We must not shirk from this duty. <Callout>The fear of the Lord establishes a hearty and unfeigned faith in God that outfits our growing children to withstand unspeakable pain and difficulty.</Callout> <Callout>What a wonderful and demanding parental vocation: calling children Godward in faith and sending them into the world to suffer as righteous followers of Jesus who take refuge in God.</Callout>

##### Conclusion

In this psalm, we learn about an important goal of parenting: character development in the fear of God. The logic of the passage is timeless and speaks to all generations and all cultures. Christian homebuilding fosters a family life of continual worship, establishing habits of worship that teach our children to continually live in the presence of God. Our most basic lesson must be to summon our children to fear God—taking his righteousness seriously, honor his glorious worthy, and relishing his unalloyed goodness. Healthy fear of God is an expression of genuine faith that results in deep character transformation. In turn, godly character represents such a dynamic change in a person's life that they can be a righteous sufferer in a hostile world. In sum, <Callout>godly parents aim for children to experience God for themselves in all his glory, his goodness, his righteousness, and his gracious deliverance.</Callout> May God help us to build hearth and home with our spiritual mantle bearing witness to the fear the Lord!

- The fear of God produces lasting change of godly character. Parents need to orient their parenting work towards character formation.
- Godly parents prepare children to be righteous sufferers. Parenting work should help prepare children to face hardship, suffering, and persecution without abandoning their faith in God.
